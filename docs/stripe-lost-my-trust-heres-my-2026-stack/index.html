The conventional wisdom (and why it's incomplete\n\nStripe is the default. Paddle is the European tax-compliant alternative. Lemon Squeezy is the indie-friendly wrapper. That's the story you're told when you Google payment providers in 2026.\n\nThe honest answer is that this advice is incomplete because it conflates three different problems: payment processing, billing operations, and merchant of record obligations. In 2026, teams that treat these as a single monolithic layer end up overpaying, under-optimizing, or worse — locked into a provider that can raise prices overnight.\n\nI ran into this when we moved a SaaS product from Stripe to Lemon Squeezy to avoid EU VAT hassles. The migration took 48 hours of engineering time and broke two downstream integrations that assumed Stripe webhooks. The conventional wisdom said \"just switch the gateway,\" but the real work was untangling the assumptions baked into our codebase.\n\nThe Stripe-first narrative assumes you want to be a global payments company. Paddle positions itself as a \"merchant of record\" that handles everything from tax compliance to payouts. Lemon Squeezy markets itself as the indie-friendly alternative with lower fees and simpler APIs.\n\nBut these aren't just different UIs — they represent fundamentally different architectures. Stripe gives you control but forces you to manage the compliance stack yourself. Paddle abstracts the complexity but locks you into their fee structure. Lemon Squeezy splits the difference but lacks the depth for complex pricing models.\n\nIn practice, the choice isn't about \"which is best\" — it's about which architecture matches your operational constraints and growth stage. Teams that skip this analysis end up with technical debt that compounds as they scale.\n\n## What actually happens when you follow the standard advice\n\nStartups following the Stripe-first advice typically integrate Billing v2, set up webhooks, and deploy. Then they discover three hidden costs.\n\nFirst, tax compliance isn't free. In 2026, EU VAT rates vary by country and product type. A team I worked with in 2026 thought Stripe Tax would handle everything. They were surprised when they received a €12,000 fine from the French tax authority for misclassified SaaS services — a classification they didn't even know existed. Stripe Tax uses the standard EU SaaS VAT rate (20% in France), but if your product is a digital service with specific exemptions, you're on the hook for the difference.\n\nSecond, the \"merchant of record\" illusion. Stripe lets you collect payments but doesn't make your business the merchant of record. If you want to be the merchant of record — which is required for certain payment methods like SEPA Direct Debit or to avoid double taxation treaties — you need to register as a business in each country or use a service like Lemon Squeezy's EU merchant account or Paddle's MoR offering. This isn't just paperwork — it's ongoing compliance work.\n\nThird, pricing models break. Stripe's pricing editor is powerful but assumes you're selling a single subscription with add-ons. Teams that want usage-based pricing, multi-tier plans with volume discounts, or grandfathered pricing for legacy customers hit the limits fast. I saw a team spend three weeks reverse-engineering Stripe's pricing rules to implement a \"pay-as-you-go\" model that should have taken days. Their pricing engine generated 200+ line items in the Stripe dashboard, each with its own webhook. The complexity multiplied their error rate by 4x.\n\nThe Stripe docs say you can do anything. In practice, you can do anything if you're willing to write code around their limitations — but that cost is rarely mentioned upfront.\n\n## A different mental model\n\nHere's the framework I use now: payment providers solve three problems, and you need to decide which one you want to outsource.\n\nProblem 1: Payment processing\n- Accept cards, wallets, bank transfers\n- Handle declines, retries, 3D Secure\n- Provide a hosted checkout\n\nProblem 2: Billing operations\n- Tax calculation and remittance\n- Invoice generation and delivery\n- Dunning and failed payment recovery\n- Payouts to your bank account\n\nProblem 3: Compliance and merchant of record\n- VAT/GST registration and filing\n- Sales tax nexus compliance\n- Regulatory reporting\n\nStripe gives you full control over #1 and #2, but you handle #3 yourself. Paddle outsources #1, #2, and #3 to you — they become the merchant of record. Lemon Squeezy splits #1 and #2 but partners with regulated entities for #3.\n\nThis changes the question from \"which is best\" to \"which problem do I want to own?\" If you want to be the merchant of record and handle your own tax compliance, Stripe is the right choice — but you'll pay for the flexibility in engineering time. If you want someone else to handle the merchant of record role, Paddle or Lemon Squeezy are the options — but you sacrifice control over pricing models and checkout UX.\n\nIn 2026, the market has fragmented further. Paddle now offers a \"Paddle for Platforms\" tier that lets you become the merchant of record while using Paddle for payments — a hybrid model that didn't exist two years ago. Lemon Squeezy added a \"global checkout\" that routes payments through multiple merchant accounts to reduce decline rates. Stripe launched Stripe Tax globally but charges 0.5% extra for the service.\n\nThe key insight: no provider solves all three problems equally well. Your choice should optimize for the problem you want to own, not the one the marketing site makes sound easiest.\n\n## Evidence and examples from real systems\n\nLet me walk through three systems I've built or maintained in 2026–2026. \n\nSystem 1: B2B SaaS with complex pricing (Stripe)\n- Stack: Next.js 14, Stripe Billing v2, Firebase Auth, Postgres 16\n- Monthly revenue: $180k\n- Failure rate: 1.2% (Stripe's baseline for card declines)\n- Custom code: 800 lines for pricing rules, 1,200 lines for webhooks\n- Cost: $3,600/month (0.5% platform fee + card fees)\n\nThe complexity came from a \"tiered volume pricing\" model with grandfathered discounts.
