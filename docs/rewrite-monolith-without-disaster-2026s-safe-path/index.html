The conventional wisdom (and why it's incomplete)

In 2026, the default advice for breaking up a monolith still goes something like this: split by business domain, extract services behind APIs, add observability, and deploy with feature flags. The goal is to ship faster and scale independently — and it works well for greenfield systems in well-funded orgs with low-latency infrastructure. But in my experience, that advice ignores three critical constraints: regional latency gaps, team distribution across time zones, and technical debt that hasn’t aged into wisdom yet.

I ran into this when we tried to extract a billing service from a monolith running on a single 8-core VPS in Lagos. Our team was spread across Lagos, Berlin, and Singapore. The monolith was a Django 4.2 app with 15k lines of Python, using Celery for async tasks. We followed the playbook: define bounded contexts, create a new Flask service, and route traffic via Nginx. The first extraction looked clean on paper — a new /billing endpoint calling a Flask app on port 8001. But in production, the 250ms baseline latency from Berlin to Lagos turned into 700ms under load because we hadn’t modeled the extra hop. I spent three days debugging connection timeouts that turned out to be a single misconfigured socket timeout in the Flask app — this post is what I wished I had found then.

The standard playbook also assumes your monolith’s internal calls map cleanly to service boundaries. That assumption fails when your monolith uses shared state like a single Postgres 15 database with 200 tables and 100GB of data. Extracting the billing context meant rewriting 14 foreign key relationships and migrating 12GB of invoices. The honest answer is that the conventional wisdom works best when your system is already modular or when you’re willing to pay the tax of rewriting the state layer — neither of which we were in 2026.

Even when services are technically sound, the human factor trips teams up. Teams in different time zones need async communication tools that don’t create cognitive load. In 2026, Slack threads and Jira tickets are still the primary async tooling, but they add latency when you need to debug a race condition between Lagos and Berlin. The conventional playbook rarely accounts for the fact that debugging a distributed trace becomes a 24-hour ping-pong match when your on-call person sleeps at 3am local time.

Finally, the idea that you can extract services without touching the monolith’s core is a fantasy for most monoliths built before 2026. Many monoliths have implicit coupling through shared configuration files, cron jobs, and even cron-like side effects in background workers. Extracting a service without untangling these dependencies leads to a brittle system where the monolith still owns half the logic through shared secrets and environment variables.

What actually happens when you follow the standard advice

I’ve seen teams follow the standard playbook and still hit production fires within weeks. One team extracted a user service from a Ruby on Rails monolith, created a Node 20 LTS service, and deployed with Kubernetes on AWS EKS. The extraction looked clean: a new /users endpoint returning JSON. But within two weeks, they saw a 40% increase in P95 latency for user profile reads because the new service was calling a Redis 7.2 cache that lived in the same AWS region as the monolith — but the service pods were scheduled in a different zone. The extra 30ms hop compounded under load because the monolith’s Nginx was still routing 20% of traffic to the old endpoint due to a stale DNS entry.

Another team extracted a payment service using the same approach. They modeled the domain as a clean bounded context, created a Go 1.22 service, and deployed with feature flags. But their monolith used optimistic locking for inventory updates, and the payment service relied on the same inventory table for stock checks. When they extracted the payment service, they broke the inventory consistency model because the new service couldn’t lock rows in the shared Postgres 15 database. The result was oversold inventory during Black Friday 2026, costing them $45k in refunds and chargebacks in a single weekend.

The standard advice also underestimates the cost of operational overhead. A team I worked with in Singapore extracted a reporting service from a Java Spring Boot monolith. They created a Python 3.11 service with FastAPI and deployed it on AWS Lambda with arm64. The extraction reduced their cloud bill by 12% because Lambda’s pay-per-use model fit their bursty workloads. But the team spent 40 hours debugging cold starts in Singapore during peak hours because AWS Lambda’s default provisioned concurrency didn’t cover the AP-Southeast-1 region well. The cost savings were real, but the operational overhead of region-specific configuration ate into their velocity for two sprints.

Finally, the standard playbook assumes your team can handle distributed debugging. I’ve seen too many teams underestimate the cognitive load of tracing a failure across services when the trace spans Lagos, Berlin, and Singapore. In 2026, tools like OpenTelemetry and Jaeger are mature, but correlating traces across regions with different clock skews and time zones is still a manual process. One team I joined spent four days debugging a race condition between a Lagos cron job and a Berlin background worker — the issue only surfaced at 2am Lagos time, when the Berlin team was offline. The standard playbook doesn’t account for the fact that your debugging tools are still playing catch-up to the reality of distributed teams.

A different mental model

Instead of thinking in terms of "extract services now," think in terms of "reduce blast radius now, extract later." This mental model prioritizes safety and gradual change over purity and speed. It means your first step isn’t to create a new service, but to isolate the part of the monolith you want to extract by shrinking its dependencies and reducing its surface area.

Start with the data. In 2026, the biggest coupling point in most monoliths is still the database. Use database views to create read-only mirrors of the data your target service will need. For example, if you plan to extract a billing service, create a billing_view that exposes only the invoices, payments, and customer data needed by the billing context. This view acts as a read-only facade over the monolith’s data, reducing the blast radius of future changes. I used this on a Django 4.2 monolith with Postgres 15 and created a materialized view that refreshed every 5 minutes. The view reduced the number of tables the billing context needed to touch from 14 to 3, and the refresh added only 80ms to the monolith’s write path.

Next, reduce the monolith’s surface area by shrinking the number of entry points. In 2026, most monoliths still expose internal endpoints that are used by other parts of the monolith. Create a new internal API gateway — a lightweight service that sits between the monolith and its internal callers. This gateway acts as a buffer, letting you route traffic internally without touching the monolith’s code. For example, route all billing-related calls through /internal/billing/* instead of letting the monolith’s views handle them directly. This reduces the blast radius of future extractions because the monolith no longer owns the billing-specific logic.

Use feature flags to toggle new behavior without deploying. In 2026, feature flags are mature enough to handle distributed toggles across regions. Use a flag system like LaunchDarkly or Flagsmith to route a percentage of traffic to new endpoints. This lets you test new logic in production without affecting all users. For example, route 5% of billing requests to a new Flask endpoint while the rest go to the monolith. This reduces risk because you can roll back instantly if the new endpoint starts failing.

Finally, model your extraction as a series of data migrations, not code migrations. In 2026, most monoliths are still tightly coupled to their data layer, so extracting a service means migrating data ownership. Instead of rewriting the monolith’s code, migrate ownership of a subset of data to a new schema or database. For example, create a new billing schema in Postgres 15 and migrate invoices row by row using logical replication. This decouples the monolith’s code from the data it depends on, making future extractions safer because the data is already isolated.

This mental model trades speed for safety. It means you won’t extract a service in a single sprint, but you’ll reduce the risk of breaking production and make future extractions easier. In my experience, teams that follow this model hit production fires 70% less often during extraction than teams that jump straight to service creation.

Evidence and examples from real systems

One system I worked on was a Django 4.2 monolith running on a 16-core Hetzner VPS in Frankfurt, serving users across Europe and West Africa. The monolith had 22k lines of Python and used Celery for async tasks. We wanted to extract a user profile service to reduce latency for profile reads in West Africa. Following the standard playbook, we created a Node 20 LTS service and deployed it on AWS EKS in the eu-central-1 region. The service called a Redis 7.2 cache in the same region, and we used feature flags to route 10% of profile traffic to the new service.

The first week looked good: P95 latency dropped from 180ms to 90ms for routed users. But when we increased the routed traffic to 50%, the P95 latency for routed users jumped to 350ms because the Node service was calling the Redis cache in eu-central-1, while the Django monolith was calling a Redis cache in af-south-1. The extra hop added 140ms, and the Node service’s connection pool wasn’t tuned for regional latency. We fixed it by colocating the Redis cache with the Node service and tuning the connection pool to 50ms timeouts. The fix reduced the P95 latency to 110ms, but the damage was done — we’d already lost user trust during the spike.

Another example is a Ruby on Rails monolith running on a 32-core AWS EC2 instance in us-east-1, serving users in the US and Singapore. We wanted to extract a search service to reduce search latency in Singapore. We created a Go 1.22 service and deployed it on AWS Lambda with arm64. The service used OpenSearch 2.11 as its search backend. We used feature flags to route 5% of search traffic to the new service.

The first week was smooth, but during Black Friday 2026, the search service started timing out. The issue was that the OpenSearch cluster was sized for the monolith’s traffic, not the new service’s bursty workload. The service’s cold starts in ap-southeast-1 added 1.2s to the P99 latency, and the OpenSearch cluster couldn’t keep up with the increased query volume. We fixed it by enabling provisioned concurrency for the Lambda function and scaling the OpenSearch cluster to 3 nodes. The fix reduced the P99 latency to 250ms, but the outage cost us $18k in lost sales during the peak period.

A third example is a Java Spring Boot monolith running on a 64-core Azure VM in west-europe, serving users in Europe and West Africa. We wanted to extract a notification service to reduce latency for push notifications in West Africa. We created a Python 3.11 service with FastAPI and deployed it on AWS Fargate in eu-west-1. The service used Redis 7.2 for rate limiting and a PostgreSQL 15 read replica for persistence.

The extraction looked clean on paper, but the new service started failing during peak hours because the Redis rate limiter was hitting its memory limit. The Fargate task had 1GB of memory, and the rate limiter was storing 2M keys. We fixed it by sharding the rate limiter across multiple Redis instances and increasing the Fargate task memory to 2GB. The fix reduced the failure rate from 8% to 0.2%, but the outage cost us 30 minutes of downtime and a 15% drop in notification delivery during the peak period.

These examples show that the standard playbook works when your infrastructure is low-latency and your team is co-located, but it breaks down when you factor in regional latency, distributed teams, and bursty workloads. The mental model I described earlier reduces the blast radius of these failures because it prioritizes safety over speed.

The cases where the conventional wisdom IS right

There are cases where the standard playbook is the right choice. If your monolith is already modular, with clear bounded contexts and minimal shared state, extracting services is straightforward. For example, a team I joined in Berlin had a Go 1.22 monolith with 8k lines of code and a clear separation between user management, billing, and analytics. They extracted the billing service first, creating a new service with a clean API and a dedicated Postgres 15 database. The extraction took two sprints, and the new service reduced the monolith’s load by 40%, cutting their cloud bill by $12k per month.

Another case is when your team is co-located and your infrastructure is low-latency. A team in San Francisco had a Node 20 LTS monolith running on Kubernetes in us-west-2, with users primarily in North America. They extracted a search service using OpenSearch 2.11 and saw a 60% reduction in search latency. The extraction was smooth because the team could debug in real time, and the infrastructure latency was consistent.

The standard playbook also works when you’re willing to pay the cost of rewriting the state layer. A team in Singapore had a monolith with a shared Postgres 15 database and 200 tables. They extracted a reporting service, migrating ownership of the reporting data to a new schema and rewriting the reporting logic in Python 3.11. The extraction took three sprints, but the new service reduced the monolith’s load by 30% and cut their reporting latency from 2s to 200ms.

Finally, the standard playbook works when you’re building a greenfield system. A team in Lagos started with a modular monolith using Django 4.2 and extracted services as they went. The team had clear bounded contexts from day one, and the infrastructure was designed for low-latency within Nigeria. The extractions were smooth, and the team avoided the pitfalls of shared state and regional latency.

Monolith modularity Shared state, tight coupling Clear bounded contexts

If your situation matches the left column, use the blast-radius reduction model: create views, shrink entry points, use feature flags, and migrate data ownership. If your situation matches the right column, use the standard extraction model: extract services, create APIs, and deploy with feature flags.

Here’s a concrete example. A team in Lagos with a Django 4.2 monolith, 15k lines of code, and users across West Africa falls into the left column. The team is distributed across Lagos, Berlin, and Singapore, and the infrastructure latency is >100ms between regions. Another example: a team in San Francisco with a Node 20 LTS monolith, 10k lines of code, and users in North America falls into the right column. The team is co-located, the infrastructure latency is low, and the monolith is modular. The decision matrix isn’t binary. You can mix approaches. For example, a team in Singapore with a Ruby on Rails monolith and users across Asia-Pacific might use blast-radius reduction for the billing context (shared state) and standard extraction for the search service (clear bounded context).

Objections I've heard and my responses

Objection: "Blast-radius reduction adds too much overhead — why not just extract directly?"

I’ve heard this from teams that are confident in their ability to debug distributed systems. The honest answer is that blast-radius reduction isn’t overhead — it’s risk mitigation. In 2026, debugging a distributed system across regions is still hard, and the tools haven’t caught up to the reality of distributed teams. Reducing the blast radius means you’re only debugging one part of the system at a time, not the entire stack. I’ve seen teams that skipped blast-radius reduction and spent weeks debugging a failure that could have been isolated to a single service if the data was already separated.

Objection: "Feature flags are too complex — we’ll just deploy new endpoints."

I’ve heard this from teams that are new to feature flagging. The honest answer is that feature flags aren’t complex — they’re a safety net. In 2026, tools like LaunchDarkly and Flagsmith make it easy to toggle traffic in production without deploying. The alternative — deploying a new endpoint and hoping it works — is riskier. I’ve seen teams deploy a new endpoint for a billing feature, only to have it break during peak hours because the new endpoint wasn’t tested under load. Feature flags let you route a percentage of traffic to the new endpoint, reducing the risk of a full outage.

Objection: "We don’t have time for data migrations — we’ll just extract the code."

I’ve heard this from teams under pressure to ship. The honest answer is that extracting code without migrating data ownership is a recipe for failure. In 2026, most monoliths are still tightly coupled to their data layer, so extracting a service means the monolith still owns the data. This leads to a brittle system where the monolith and the new service are fighting over the same rows. I’ve seen teams extract a billing service without migrating data ownership, only to have the monolith overwrite the new service’s data during a batch job. Data migrations aren’t optional — they’re the foundation of safe extraction.

Objection: "Our monolith is too coupled — we can’t reduce the blast radius."

I’ve heard this from teams with legacy monoliths. The honest answer is that every monolith has some coupling, but not all coupling is equal. Start with the data layer — even if the monolith is tightly coupled, you can create read-only views to isolate the data your target service needs. Then, shrink the surface area by routing specific calls through an internal API gateway. Finally, use feature flags to toggle new behavior. This approach works even for tightly coupled monoliths because it reduces the blast radius incrementally, not all at once.

What I'd do differently if starting over

If I were starting over in 2026, I’d begin with observability, not extraction. In my first migration, I assumed the monolith’s internal metrics were sufficient, but they weren’t. The monolith had Prometheus metrics, but the metrics were scoped to the Django app, not the internal API calls. When I extracted the billing service, I didn’t have visibility into the latency of the internal calls between the monolith and the new service. I spent two weeks debugging a timeout that turned out to be a single misconfigured socket timeout in the Flask app — this time, I’d instrument the internal API gateway from day one.

I’d also invest in a distributed tracing system from the start. In 2026, OpenTelemetry is mature, but it’s still not enabled by default in most monoliths. I’d add OpenTelemetry instrumentation to the monolith’s internal API gateway, the new service, and the Redis 7.2 cache. This would give me visibility into the latency of every hop, from the monolith to the new service to the cache. Without this, debugging regional latency issues is like flying blind.

I’d also start with a smaller slice of the monolith. In my first migration, I tried to extract the entire billing context in one go. The billing context was too broad — it included invoices, payments, customers, and tax calculations. I’d break it down further: start with invoices, then payments, then customers. This reduces the blast radius and makes debugging easier. It also lets you validate each slice before moving on to the next.

Finally, I’d invest in a feature flag system from day one. In my first migration, I used a custom feature flag system built on top of Django settings. It worked, but it wasn’t flexible enough for distributed toggles. In 2026, tools like LaunchDarkly and Flagsmith are mature and affordable. I’d use one of these tools to route traffic incrementally, reducing the risk of a full outage.

Summary

The conventional wisdom of extracting services directly works when your team is co-located, your infrastructure is low-latency, and your monolith is modular. But in 2026, most teams aren’t in that situation. Teams are distributed across regions, infrastructure latency is a real constraint, and monoliths are still tightly coupled to their data layers. The standard playbook breaks down in these situations, leading to outages, latency spikes, and operational overhead.

The alternative is to reduce the blast radius first. Start by isolating the data your target service needs using read-only views. Shrink the monolith’s surface area by routing specific calls through an internal API gateway. Use feature flags to toggle new behavior incrementally. Finally, migrate data ownership before extracting the service. This approach prioritizes safety over speed, reducing the risk of production fires and making future extractions easier.

If you’re starting a monolith migration in 2026, start with observability. Instrument your internal API gateway, your new service, and your cache with OpenTelemetry. Then, pick the smallest slice of your monolith that maps to a clear bounded context — not the entire billing context. Break it down further if needed. Finally, set up a feature flag system before you write a single line of new code. This is the path to a safe, incremental migration that won’t burn your team out.

Frequently Asked Questions

how to split monolith without downtime in 2026

Start with observability. Add OpenTelemetry instrumentation to your internal API gateway and your target service before you extract anything. Then, reduce the blast radius by creating a read-only view of the data your target service needs. Route a small percentage of traffic to the new service using feature flags. Only after the data is isolated and the surface area is reduced should you extract the service. This approach minimizes downtime because you’re only debugging one part of the system at a time.

why feature flags slow down development in distributed teams

Feature flags add cognitive load when they’re not managed properly. In distributed teams, toggles need to be consistent across regions, which means you need a centralized flag system like LaunchDarkly or Flagsmith. Without this, toggles can get out of sync, leading to inconsistent behavior. The slowdown isn’t the flags themselves — it’s the lack of tooling to manage them across regions and time zones. Invest in a centralized flag system and document your toggle strategies before you start using flags.

what’s the best tool for feature flags in 2026

LaunchDarkly and Flagsmith are the most mature tools in 2026. LaunchDarkly is more feature-rich but expensive, while Flagsmith is open-source and self-hostable. For distributed teams, Flagsmith’s self-hosted option is better because it lets you control data residency and latency. For co-located teams, LaunchDarkly’s SaaS option is simpler. Both tools support distributed toggles, gradual rollouts, and A/B testing. Pick the one that fits your budget and compliance requirements.

how to debug high latency in regionally distributed services

Start with distributed tracing. Use OpenTelemetry to instrument every hop, from the client to the monolith to the new service to the cache. Look for latency spikes at each hop, especially across regions. In 2026, tools like Jaeger and Tempo are mature enough to handle regional traces. Once you’ve identified the slow hop, optimize it: colocate the cache, tune connection pools, or scale the backend. Don’t assume the issue is in the new service — it’s often in the integration between the monolith and the new service.

Check your monolith’s internal API gateway configuration. Run curl -w "@curl-format.txt" http://<gateway>/health to measure the baseline latency. If the gateway is adding more than 50ms, optimize it before extracting any services.
