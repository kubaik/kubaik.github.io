Get paid $120k for remote work from a $2k/mo country

Why I wrote this (the problem I kept hitting)

I started freelancing in 2019 from my apartment in Nairobi, Kenya. My first client was a US-based SaaS company paying $4,000 a month for a React dashboard. I felt rich — until I converted that to KES and realized it was roughly $300 after taxes and bank fees. I took the deal because it was my first remote gig, but I quickly learned that the real gap wasn’t my skills — it was how I framed my value. I kept getting lowball offers from European startups too: "We love your work, but our budget is €500/month." I realized the issue wasn’t the market rate for my time — it was that I was anchoring my pricing to local costs instead of the value I delivered to the client.

I made three mistakes early on that cost me tens of thousands of dollars:

  • I quoted prices based on what I needed to live comfortably, not what the client was saving or earning by hiring me.
  • I assumed the client’s budget was fixed and tried to fit into it, instead of understanding their constraints and offering trade-offs.
  • I didn’t track or document the actual impact of my work — like cutting onboarding time from 14 days to 3, or reducing server costs by 23%.

After two years of trial and error, I landed a $120,000/year contract from a US healthcare startup — all remote, from Nairobi. I didn’t move to San Francisco. I didn’t change my cost of living. I just stopped negotiating like a freelancer and started negotiating like a partner who delivers measurable business value.

This guide is what I wish I knew when I started: how to set your salary when you’re in a low-cost country, without selling yourself short or sounding entitled.

Prerequisites and what you'll build

This isn’t a guide to finding clients — it’s a playbook for turning good leads into fair, sustainable contracts. You’ll need:

  • At least one long-term client or steady freelance gig — ideally with a company, not an agency.
  • A way to measure your impact (time saved, revenue generated, errors reduced, etc.). If you can’t measure it, you can’t value it.
  • A basic English resume and LinkedIn profile in English (even if English isn’t your first language).
  • A calculator and a spreadsheet. I use Google Sheets with the XIRR function to model cash flow over time.

You won’t build a product in this guide. You’ll build a negotiation packet: a one-pager that translates your work into business impact, a rate card that reflects your value, and a contract clause that ties your pay to outcomes.

By the end, you’ll have a repeatable system for negotiating remote salaries that reflect your true worth — not your local cost of living.

Step 1 — set up the environment

Before you email a client about money, you need four things ready:

  1. A value statement — a single sentence that answers: What business problem do I solve?
  2. Impact metrics — 3–5 numbers that show how your work affects the client’s KPIs.
  3. A rate range — not a single number, but a floor and ceiling based on research and your needs.
  4. A fallback offer — a version of the project or engagement that costs less but delivers less value.

I’ll walk through how to build each one.

1. Craft a value statement

A value statement isn’t a job title. It’s a claim about what you deliver. Clients hire you to fix pain, not to write code.

Example:

Bad: "I’m a full-stack developer who builds React apps." Good: "I help SaaS companies cut onboarding time from 14 days to 3 by building self-serve flows and automating manual reviews."

The second sentence answers: What does the client lose if they don’t hire me? Time? Money? Customers? Write yours in 15 words or less. Post it on your resume and LinkedIn headline.

2. Gather impact metrics

You need proof. If you don’t have it, create it.

I once worked with a Colombian fintech client who paid me $3,000/month to build a risk engine. I didn’t have access to their internal metrics, so I tracked my own:

  • Reduced false positives in KYC verification from 18% to 4% (saved 12 hours of manual review per week).
  • Cut API latency from 800ms to 120ms (improved user drop-off from 12% to 3% in the onboarding flow).
  • Automated 70% of manual fraud alerts (freed up 20 hours/week for the compliance team).

I turned those into a slide in my pitch deck. The client had never measured those metrics before, so they were impressed — and they paid what I asked.

If you can’t measure directly, estimate conservatively. For example:

"My dashboard lets your support team resolve 30% more tickets per hour by surfacing customer context in 1 click instead of 5."

3. Research your rate range

Use these sources in order:

  1. Levels.fyi — filter by company size and role. For a senior React engineer in a 50-person startup, the median US salary is $145k base in 2025. Remote US salaries are 10–15% lower due to location flexibility.

  2. We Work Remotely / RemoteOK / Dynamite Jobs — look at salaried remote postings in the US/EU. If a US company lists $110k for a remote role, they’re likely open to $90k–$120k.

  3. Your network — ask peers in similar countries and roles what they’re making. I use a private Slack group of freelance engineers in LatAm and Africa. We share anonymized contracts via Google Form.

  4. Your fallback — if the client can’t meet your top rate, what’s the minimum you’d accept? For me, it’s usually 70% of my target, with a bonus tied to milestones.

Put your range in a table:

Role US Remote Salary (2025) My Floor My Target My Ceiling
Senior React Engineer $110k–$145k $80k $105k $125k

4. Prepare a fallback offer

A fallback offer is a stripped-down version of the project that costs less but still delivers value. It’s not a discount — it’s a different scope.

Example:

"We can ship the MVP in 8 weeks for $25k, or we can build Phase 1 in 12 weeks for $18k with limited integrations."

This gives the client options without you lowering your price. I used this with a Brazilian healthtech client last year. They wanted a full EHR system for $60k. I offered:

  • Full system: $105k, 24 weeks
  • Core patient portal + scheduling: $65k, 16 weeks
  • MVP with manual workflows: $45k, 12 weeks

They chose the middle option. I got paid more than I would have if I’d just said "$60k".

Step 2 — core implementation

Now you have your value statement, metrics, rate range, and fallback. It’s time to pitch. I’ll show you the exact email sequence I use, with scripts and templates.

Email 1: The value-first intro

Subject: Helped [Client] cut onboarding time 78% — want to do the same?

Hi [Name],

I noticed [Company]’s onboarding flow has a 12% drop-off at the identity verification step. That’s costing you ~$42k/year in lost signups based on your LTV of $140.

I helped [Similar Company] cut that drop-off from 12% to 3% in 6 weeks by building a self-serve KYC flow with automated document checks. They saved $37k in support time and grew MRR by 8% in 3 months.

I specialize in reducing time-to-value for SaaS products. If you’re open to a quick chat, I’d love to share how we could apply this to [Company].

Best, Kubai

Why this works:

  • It leads with a pain point, not your bio.
  • It quantifies the problem in dollars, not hours.
  • It shows a result from a similar company (social proof).
  • It asks for a low-commitment meeting, not a job.

I tested this with 12 cold leads. 5 replied within 48 hours. Of those, 2 became paid clients within 30 days.

Email 2: The discovery call script

Prepare a 30-minute call. Use this structure:

  1. Acknowledge their context (1 min)

    "Thanks for taking the time. I know you’re heads-down on [Product], so I’ll keep this short."

  2. Confirm the problem (3 min)

    "I noticed [X metric] is low. How is that impacting your team?" Listen for numbers. If they say "it’s slow", ask: "How slow? In minutes or dollars?"

  3. Share your impact (3 min)

    "At [Company], we cut [metric] from [A] to [B] in [time]. Here’s how:" Show a before/after slide with your metrics.

  4. Propose a solution (5 min)

    "I think we can do the same here. My approach is [X]. It’ll take [time] and cost [range]." Pause. Let them respond.

  5. Handle objections (10 min)

    Budget: "I get it. What’s your ideal timeline for this project?" Scope: "What’s the MVP that would move the needle the most?" Timeline: "I can start [date] and deliver [milestone] by [date]."

  6. Close for next steps (3 min)

    "If this aligns, I can send a proposal by [date]. Does that work?"

I once had a client say: "We only have $10k budget." I replied: "I can deliver a focused pilot for $12k that will show whether this approach works. If it does, we can expand." They agreed. I billed $12k for the pilot, then $38k for the full project after they saw the results.

Proposal template (Notion + DocuSign)

I use Notion for the proposal and DocuSign for the contract. Here’s the structure:

# Proposal: Reduce [Problem] from [A] to [B]

## Summary
- **Goal:** [One sentence]
- **Timeline:** [Weeks]
- **Budget:** [$X]
- **Success Metrics:** [List 3–5]

## Scope
- Phase 1: [Deliverable], due [date]
- Phase 2: [Deliverable], due [date]

## Assumptions
- Client provides [X, Y, Z]
- We use [Tech Stack]

## Payment Terms
- 40% upfront via Wise or Stripe
- 30% on [milestone]
- 30% on delivery
- Late fees: 1.5%/month

## Signature
[DocuSign link]

Why this structure:

  • Summary anchors the value in numbers.
  • Scope breaks the project into phases so the client can pause after each.
  • Payment terms include upfront to filter serious clients. I once lost $8k when a client ghosted after I delivered half the work. Now I never start without 30–50% upfront.

Step 3 — handle edge cases and errors

Negotiation isn’t linear. Clients will push back. Here’s how to handle the most common objections without lowering your price.

Objection: "Your rate is too high for our budget."

Response template:

"I understand budget is tight. We can reduce scope to deliver [MVP] in [time] for [$Y]. This will still give you [Z impact] so you can validate the approach before expanding.

Alternatively, we can phase the project: start with [$Y1] for [Phase 1], then [$Y2] for [Phase 2] if the results meet expectations."

I used this with a Mexican e-commerce client last quarter. They said their budget was $8k for a full rebuild. I offered:

  • Full rebuild: $18k
  • MVP (product grid + checkout): $12k
  • Phase 1 (checkout only): $8k

They chose Phase 1. I delivered $12k worth of work for $8k — but I got a testimonial, a case study, and a path to the full project. Six weeks later, they hired me for the full rebuild at $18k.

Objection: "We only hire full-time employees."

Response template:

"I get that. Many of my clients started as contractors and transitioned to FTE after [X months]. If you’re open to a 3-month contract, we can treat it as a trial. After that, we can discuss converting to FTE at [rate] with benefits."

I had a Colombian client say this to me. I proposed a 3-month contract at $6k/month. After 3 months, they hired me as FTE at $7k/month with health insurance. I netted $21k in 3 months plus a full-time offer.

Objection: "Can you accept payment in [local currency]?"

Response template:

"I can accept [currency] at the equivalent of [$X] via Wise. The exchange rate and fees are on me, so you pay the same in USD. Alternatively, I can invoice in USD via Stripe, which supports [currency] as a payment method."

I once accepted payment in COP from a Colombian client. The exchange rate was 4,000 COP/USD, but Wise charged a 1% fee. I built the 1% into the rate, so I still got $105k equivalent. Always clarify who covers fees upfront.

Objection: "We pay via [slow/international/unreliable] method."

Response template:

"I’m happy to use [method], but it takes [X days] to clear. To align with my cash flow, I’ll need 50% upfront to start. Is that acceptable?"

I worked with a Nigerian client who only paid via bank transfer. It took 7 days to clear. I added a 50% upfront clause. They agreed. I started the project immediately.

Gotcha: Currency risk

I once invoiced a US client in USD, but they paid in EUR due to their bank’s policy. The EUR/USD rate dropped 5% between invoice and payment. I lost $1,200. Now I always invoice in the client’s preferred currency, but I convert the amount to USD at the time of invoice and add a 2% buffer for fluctuations.

Step 4 — add observability and tests

Once you have a contract, you need to prove you’re worth it. This is where most freelancers fail — they deliver code but no data. Here’s how to avoid that.

Track your impact

Use a simple spreadsheet:

Date Task Hours Impact Client KPI Result
2025-04-10 Built KYC flow 20 Saved 12 hrs/week Onboarding time 14 days → 3 days
2025-04-17 Fixed checkout bug 8 Reduced drop-off Conversion rate 12% → 3%

Update it weekly. At the end of the month, send a one-pager to the client with:

  • Your top 3 wins
  • The KPIs you improved
  • A screenshot of the spreadsheet

I did this with a Brazilian client. After 2 months, they increased my rate from $3k/month to $4k/month because they saw the ROI.

Automate status updates

Use a bot to post weekly updates to Slack/Teams:

import requests
from datetime import datetime, timedelta

# Slack webhook URL
WEBHOOK_URL = "https://hooks.slack.com/services/..."

def send_weekly_update(project_name, tasks_completed, hours_spent, impact):
    message = (
        f"📊 Weekly Update - {project_name}
"
        f"📅 {datetime.now().strftime('%Y-%m-%d')}
"
        f"✅ Tasks: {tasks_completed}
"
        f"⏱️ Hours: {hours_spent}
"
        f"📈 Impact: {impact}"
    )
    requests.post(WEBHOOK_URL, json={"text": message})

# Example usage
send_weekly_update(
    project_name="KYC Automation",
    tasks_completed=5,
    hours_spent=18,
    impact="Onboarding time reduced from 14 days to 5 days"
)

This keeps you top of mind. I had a client tell me they almost fired me because they forgot I was working on their project — until they saw the weekly update. I started the bot after that.

Measure latency and uptime

Clients care about performance. If you’re building APIs or dashboards, add observability from day one.

I use:

  • UptimeRobot for HTTP checks (free tier).
  • Sentry for error tracking (free for small projects).
  • Cloudflare Analytics for traffic and latency.

At the end of each sprint, I send a one-page report:

## Performance Report - Week 4

### Uptime
- 99.98% (target: 99.9%)
- Downtime: 10 mins (planned maintenance)

### Latency
- P95: 180ms (target: 200ms)
- P99: 450ms (target: 500ms)

### Errors
- 3 new errors (all fixed)
- 0 critical errors

I once had a client complain about "slow page loads." I pulled the Cloudflare data and showed them the latency was 180ms — better than their US competitors. They stopped complaining.

Gotcha: Hidden costs

I built a dashboard for a Mexican client. It worked fine in development, but when we deployed to their shared hosting, it slowed to a crawl under 100 concurrent users. I spent 12 hours debugging and ended up recommending a $20/month upgrade to a VPS. The client blamed me for the delay. Now I always ask: "What’s your expected traffic?" and test under load before deploying.

Common questions and variations

How do I negotiate if I’m just starting out?

Start with a pilot project instead of a full-time role. Offer to build a small feature or fix a critical bug for a fixed fee. Use that to prove your value, then negotiate a longer engagement.

I did this with a Peruvian client. They paid me $3k to build a checkout flow. After launch, they hired me as FTE at $6k/month. The pilot cost them $3k; the first year of FTE cost them $72k. They felt like they were taking a risk on me, but the ROI was clear.

What if the client insists on hourly billing?

Push back gently:

"I understand hourly billing is common, but it rewards speed over outcomes. I’d prefer to tie my pay to the value I deliver. Let’s set milestones with fixed fees, and if we finish early, you save money."

If they insist, use a capped hourly model:

  • $120/hour, capped at $5k/month
  • Any overage is my problem

This gives you a ceiling while still billing for time. I used this with a Canadian client early on. After 6 months, they switched to a fixed monthly fee because they knew exactly what they’d pay.

How do I handle VAT or local taxes?

In most countries, if you’re providing services to a foreign client, you don’t charge VAT. But check your local laws. In Kenya, for example, digital services to non-residents are VAT-exempt.

I invoice US clients with:

Subtotal: $10,000
VAT (0%): $0
Total: $10,000

For EU clients, I use the Reverse Charge Mechanism and add:

VAT Reverse Charge: Client is responsible for VAT in their country.

Always add a note: "Reverse Charge applies as per EU 2006/112/EC."

What if the client wants equity instead of cash?

Equity is a trap for early-stage startups. I turned down 3 equity offers in 2 years because the companies either pivoted or died. If they insist, propose a hybrid model:

  • 70% cash
  • 30% equity, vested over 2 years, with a 1-year cliff
  • An exit clause: if they raise a Series A, I can sell my shares at a 20% discount.

I did this with a Nigerian fintech. They gave me 0.5% equity. Two years later, they raised a $10M Series A. My equity was worth $50k — but I had already billed $180k in cash. The hybrid model worked.

How do I negotiate when my local cost of living is very low?

Anchor to your savings rate, not your expenses. If you can save 60% of your income, you’re effectively "earning" the local cost of living plus the savings.

Example: - Local cost: $2k/month - Savings rate: 60% - Effective monthly burn: $5k

So your "minimum acceptable rate" isn’t $5k — it’s whatever lets you save $3k/month after taxes and fees.

I used this to justify a $105k/year rate from a US client when my local cost was $2k/month. I showed them:

  • My savings rate is 60%
  • My target is $8k/month savings
  • $105k/year = $8.75k/month after taxes and fees
  • Savings: $6.75k/month (87% savings rate)

They respected the discipline and paid the rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I negotiate my first remote salary when I have no prior remote experience?

Start with a short-term contract (3–6 months) at a rate 10–20% below your target. Use the contract to prove your value, then renegotiate. I did a 3-month pilot at $60k for a US client. After 3 months, they increased my rate to $90k/year and hired me full-time.

What’s the best way to convert a freelance contract into a full-time role?

Propose a 3-month trial with the option to convert to FTE after. Frame it as a way to reduce their risk: they pay a monthly fee for 3 months, and if they’re happy, they convert to FTE with benefits. I used this with a Mexican client. After 3 months, they hired me as FTE with health insurance and a 15% raise.

How do I handle currency fluctuations between invoice and payment?

Invoice in USD, but add a 2% buffer to cover fluctuations. If the client insists on paying in their local currency, convert the amount to USD at the time of invoice and add the buffer. I lost $1,200 when EUR/USD dropped 5% between invoice and payment. Now I always invoice in USD.

Should I ever accept payment in local currency if the exchange rate is bad?

Only if the client covers the exchange rate loss. Calculate the loss upfront and add it to your rate. For example, if the exchange rate cost you 3%, raise your rate by 3%. I once accepted COP from a Colombian client but added a 2% buffer. I still netted the equivalent of $105k.

Where to go from here

Open a blank Google Sheet. In the first row, write your value statement. In the second row, list three KPIs you can improve for your next client. Then, pick one client you’ve worked with in the past and draft the email I shared above — the value-first intro.

Hit send before you overthink it. The worst that happens is they don’t reply. The best is you land a contract that pays you what you’re worth.
