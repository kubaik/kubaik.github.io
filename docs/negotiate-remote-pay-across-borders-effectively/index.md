# Negotiate remote pay across borders effectively

A colleague asked me about this last week and I realised I couldn't explain it cleanly. Writing this post forced me to think it through properly — which is usually how it goes.

## Why I wrote this (the problem I kept hitting)

When I first started freelancing as an engineer from Kenya, I was thrilled to land contracts with clients in the US, Brazil, and Germany. But negotiating pay was a struggle. Clients would ask, "What’s your rate?" and I’d freeze. If I aimed too high, I worried they’d walk away. If I aimed too low, I’d kick myself later. The worst part? Some clients would flat-out say, "But your cost of living is lower, why should we pay as much as we would in New York?"

I spent months learning how to navigate these conversations. I tested different approaches, some of which backfired badly. The worst was when I tried quoting hourly rates without understanding how the client calculated budgets — I lost a $10k project because I misunderstood their expectations. This post is the guide I wish I had when I started.

## Prerequisites and what you'll build

By the end of this article, you’ll be able to:

1. Articulate your value to a client without tying it to your cost of living.
2. Use data to anchor your salary or rate negotiations.
3. Handle pushback from clients who want to pay less due to your location.

You don’t need to have negotiation experience, but you should have a good grasp of your skills and the type of work you’re offering. It also helps if you have at least one completed project or portfolio to showcase your work.

---

## Step 1 — Research the market rate (and your worth)

### Why

Clients aren’t going to pay you what you’re worth — they’re going to pay you what you can justify. That starts with understanding the market rate for your role. This isn’t about what *you* think you deserve; it’s about what similar professionals are charging for similar work.

### How

1. **Start with public salary data**: Use platforms like Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, or Payscale to find salary ranges for your role in the client’s country. For example, as of 2026, a mid-level software engineer in the US earns between $100,000 and $140,000 annually.
2. **Check freelance marketplaces**: Look at platforms like Upwork, Toptal, or Fiverr. Search for profiles in your field and note their rates. For instance, a senior backend developer on Upwork might charge $50–$100/hour, depending on expertise.
3. **Ask peers**: Join communities like Dev.to, Indie Hackers, or Reddit’s r/freelance. Ask directly: "What are you charging for Python backend development in the US market?"
4. **Calculate your floor**: Determine your minimum acceptable rate based on your living costs and desired savings. For example, if you need $2,000/month to cover expenses and want to save $1,000/month, you’ll need to earn at least $3,000 monthly. Divide this by your available working hours to get your base hourly rate.

### Pro Tip

Don’t let your local rates anchor you. If developers in the US charge $70/hour, your location shouldn’t automatically mean you charge $20/hour. You’re selling solutions, not just hours.

---

## Step 2 — Position your value, not your location

### Why

Clients often default to thinking about cost when outsourcing to lower-cost regions. Your job is to shift their focus from your geography to your expertise and the impact of your work.

### How

1. **Frame your expertise**: Instead of saying, "I’m a developer from [your country]," say, "I specialize in optimizing backend systems to handle high traffic. My last project reduced API response times by 40%."
2. **Highlight outcomes**: Use metrics to show your impact. For instance, "I helped an e-commerce client increase their checkout conversion rate by 15%, resulting in $50k in additional monthly revenue."
3. **Address cost of living upfront**: If clients bring up your location, have a clear response. For example: "While I live in a lower-cost region, my rates reflect the value and expertise I bring to the table, which aligns with global standards in this field."

### Example Script

Here’s a script I’ve used successfully:

"I understand that location often comes up in discussions about rates. My focus is on delivering outcomes. For instance, on a recent project, I implemented a caching solution that reduced page load times by 70%, which directly improved user retention. My rate reflects the value of these results."  

### Gotcha

Don’t apologize for your location. I made this mistake early on, saying things like, "I know salaries here are lower, so I can adjust my rate." This just signals to the client that they can push you down further.

## Step 3 — Anchor the conversation with data

### Why

If you don’t control the narrative, the client will. Anchoring the negotiation with data puts you in the driver’s seat and makes your rate feel less arbitrary.

### How

1. **Present a range**: Use the research from Step 1 to propose a range. For example, "For a senior React developer role, the market rate is $60–$100/hour. Based on my experience, I’m proposing $80/hour."
2. **Use comparisons**: Show how your rate is a bargain. For instance, "Hiring a full-time developer in the US for this role would cost $10,000/month. My rate is $6,000/month, which provides significant savings without compromising quality."
3. **Showcase efficiency**: Emphasize how your skills save time. "Because I specialize in optimizing database performance, I can deliver results faster than a junior developer learning on the job."

### Pro Tip

Always start with the *higher* end of your range. Most clients will negotiate down, so you need room to offer a discount without underselling yourself.

---

## Step 4 — Handle edge cases and errors

### Why

Not every client will accept your rate without pushback. Some will try to negotiate aggressively, while others might ghost you altogether. Knowing how to respond can save you time and frustration.

### How

1. **Dealing with lowballers**: If a client offers a rate far below your minimum, don’t be afraid to walk away. For example, if your floor is $30/hour and they offer $15/hour, respond with: "I appreciate your offer, but my minimum rate is $30/hour due to the level of expertise and results I provide. If your budget changes, I’d be happy to revisit this."
2. **Handling "We’ll find someone cheaper"**: Some clients may threaten to hire someone else. Respond calmly: "I understand. However, I believe my experience ensures the project will be completed efficiently and to a high standard, saving you costs in the long run."
3. **Ghosting**: If a client stops responding after you propose a rate, follow up once with a polite message: "Hi [Name], I wanted to follow up on my proposal. Please let me know if you have any questions or concerns." If they still don’t reply, move on.

### Gotcha

I once agreed to a lower-than-ideal rate because the client promised "lots of future work." Guess what? That future work never came. Always negotiate based on the current project, not vague promises.

---

## Step 5 — Add written agreements and milestones

### Why

A clear contract protects both you and the client. It sets expectations, reduces misunderstandings, and ensures you get paid for your work.

### How

1. **Use templates**: Tools like Bonsai or AND.CO offer free contract templates tailored for freelancers. Customize one for your project.
2. **Define deliverables**: Specify exactly what you’ll deliver and when. For example: "Deliver a fully functional e-commerce backend with payment integration by March 15."
3. **Set milestones**: Break the project into phases with payment tied to each milestone. For instance:

| Milestone                  | Deliverable                        | Payment  |
|----------------------------|------------------------------------|----------|
| Initial setup              | Project plan and architecture doc | 20%      |
| MVP delivery               | Working prototype                 | 50%      |
| Final delivery             | Tested and deployed application   | 30%      |

4. **Include payment terms**: Specify the payment method, currency, and deadlines. For example: "Payment will be made via Wise in USD within 7 days of receiving the invoice."

### Pro Tip

Insist on an upfront payment (at least 20%) to protect yourself against non-payment.

## Common questions and variations

### How do I handle clients who want to pay in their local currency?
If the client insists on paying in their currency, use a service like Wise or Payoneer to convert it to your preferred currency. Build a buffer into your rate to account for exchange rate fluctuations — for example, add 5% if their currency is volatile.

### Why do clients question my rate when I’m from a lower-cost country?
Clients often associate location with cost. Your job is to shift the focus to your skills and the value you bring. Use data, examples, and confidence to demonstrate why your rate is justified.

### What if I’m just starting out and don’t have a portfolio?
Focus on smaller gigs to build your portfolio. Platforms like Upwork or Fiverr can help you get your first few clients. Charge slightly below market rate initially, but only for a limited time to build a track record.

### When should I agree to a lower rate?
Only agree to a lower rate if the project offers non-monetary value, like learning a new skill or building a key portfolio piece. Otherwise, stick to your minimum rate.

## Advanced Edge Cases I Personally Encountered

### The "Time Zone Discount" Argument
One client in California argued that since I was based in Kenya, my working hours wouldn’t align with theirs, so I should offer a "discount" to compensate for the inconvenience. It was tempting to simply accept it because I did feel slightly guilty about the time zone gap. However, I quickly realized that this wasn’t a valid reason to lower my rate — my productivity and deliverables weren’t tied to whether we could have real-time calls every day. I addressed the concern by offering scheduled overlapping hours for essential discussions (e.g., 7 PM EAT to 9 PM EAT) while emphasizing that asynchronous communication was more efficient. The result? I stuck to my $60/hour rate, and the client agreed after a one-week trial proved my work was worth it.

### "We’ll Pay You in Equity"
A tech startup in Toronto once offered me a lower-than-market rate, claiming they’d make up for it with equity in their company. This wasn’t inherently a bad deal, but I learned to ask the right questions: What’s the valuation of the company? What’s the vesting schedule? How much equity are you offering, and what’s the exit plan? After digging deeper, I realized the company had no immediate revenue model and was essentially banking on a high-risk future. I passed on the opportunity and instead took a fully paid project with a Brazilian client. That startup failed within six months, reinforcing my decision.

### The "We Can Train You" Trap
In Colombia, I worked with a client who wanted me to learn an unfamiliar tech stack (Elixir) for their project. They framed it as an opportunity, but the rate they offered didn’t reflect the extra learning curve. I countered with this: "I’m happy to learn Elixir, but this will require additional ramp-up time. My rate for this learning phase is $50/hour instead of my usual $65/hour, as I’ll be allocating time for upskilling. Once I’m fully productive, we can adjust back to my standard rate." They agreed, and I used a combination of Elixir School and Exercism to get up to speed within two weeks.

## Integration with Real Tools (with Code Snippets)

### 1. **Using Wise API v2 for Cross-Border Payments**
One of the challenges I faced was ensuring I got paid in my local currency while avoiding outrageous fees and bad exchange rates. Wise (formerly TransferWise) has a robust API that lets you automate the invoicing and payment process.

Here’s a basic Python snippet to generate a payment request using the Wise API v2:

```python
import requests

API_TOKEN = 'your_wise_api_token'
BASE_URL = 'https://api.wise.com/v2'

def create_quote(source_currency, target_currency, amount):
    url = f"{BASE_URL}/quotes"
    headers = {
        'Authorization': f'Bearer {API_TOKEN}',
        'Content-Type': 'application/json'
    }
    payload = {
        "sourceCurrency": source_currency,
        "targetCurrency": target_currency,
        "sourceAmount": amount
    }
    response = requests.post(url, headers=headers, json=payload)
    return response.json()

quote = create_quote("USD", "KES", 1000)
print(f"Quote ID: {quote['id']} - Rate: {quote['rate']}")
```

With this, you can automate generating quotes and calculate your actual earnings in local currency.

### 2. **Upwork API v1.6 for Proposal Management**
If you use Upwork, automating proposal tracking can save time. Here’s an example of fetching your active proposals using the Upwork API v1.6:

```python
from upwork import Client

public_key = 'your_public_key'
secret_key = 'your_secret_key'

client = Client(public_key, secret_key)
proposals = client.proposals.get_active()

for proposal in proposals['proposals']:
    print(f"Job: {proposal['title']}, Rate: {proposal['rate']}")
```

By integrating this into your workflow, you can monitor and refine your active proposals for better client targeting.

### 3. **Payoneer API for Invoice Management**
Payoneer’s API is another lifesaver for managing cross-border payments. Here’s an example of creating an invoice using their latest SDK (v3.1):

```python
import requests

PAYONEER_API_KEY = 'your_api_key'
BASE_URL = 'https://api.payoneer.com/v3.1'

def create_invoice(amount, currency, description):
    url = f"{BASE_URL}/invoices"
    headers = {
        'Authorization': f'Bearer {PAYONEER_API_KEY}',
        'Content-Type': 'application/json'
    }
    payload = {
        "amount": amount,
        "currency": currency,
        "description": description
    }
    response = requests.post(url, headers=headers, json=payload)
    return response.json()

invoice = create_invoice(500, "USD", "Backend development services")
print(f"Invoice ID: {invoice['id']} - Status: {invoice['status']}")
```

These tools helped me streamline my negotiations, ensuring I spent less time chasing payments and more time delivering value.
