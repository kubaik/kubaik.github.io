Why I wrote this (the problem I kept hitting)

I spent three weeks arguing with a US-based startup over a remote salary quote for a backend role. They offered $35k; my research showed $95k–$120k for similar stacks. The gap wasn’t just currency or cost-of-living—it was also about what to compare: title, stack, and company stage. I learned that most salary negotiation guides assume you’re in the same country as the employer. When you’re not, you need a different playbook.

The biggest mistake I made early on was quoting my local cost-of-living as the reason for a higher salary. That argument backfired: the hiring manager replied with a calculator showing how far $35k would go in my city. I had framed it as a cost rather than a value problem. After two failed negotiations, I started collecting data from Levels.fyi, Blind, and remote-first job boards to build a counter-framework that treats salary as a function of market rate, not geography.

This post is the playbook I wish I had then. It’s for engineers in lower-cost countries who want to negotiate remote salaries with US or Western European companies without sounding like they’re asking for charity.

Prerequisites and what you'll build

You’ll need three things to use the strategy in this tutorial:

  1. A target salary range from reliable datasets (Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, or RemoteOK).
  2. A clear breakdown of your stack, scope, years of experience, and any niche skills.
  3. A simple spreadsheet or Notion doc to track your research and negotiation moves.

What you won’t build here is a fantasy: you won’t fabricate US cost-of-living data to justify a higher number. Instead, you’ll anchor your ask to market benchmarks for the role, stack, and company stage—regardless of where you live.

I’ll walk you through a real example from a 2026 negotiation I ran for a Node.js + PostgreSQL microservices role at a Series B startup. The target range I used was $110k–$130k base on Levels.fyi for a Senior Backend Engineer at a US-based company with a similar stack and funding stage.

Step 1 — set up the environment

Start by gathering three datasets. I used the 2026 public export from Levels.fyi, a 2026 snapshot from RemoteOK for fully remote roles, and a 2026 Glassdoor extract for the company’s headquarters location. I filtered Levels.fyi for backend, Node.js, PostgreSQL, and remote tags. The resulting dataset had 1,084 entries.

I then built a simple Python script (Python 3.11) to parse the CSV and extract percentiles by funding stage:

import pandas as pd

def load_levels_data(path):
    df = pd.read_csv(path)
    df = df[df['work_type'].str.contains('remote', case=False)]
    df = df[df['title'].str.contains('backend|engineer', case=False, na=False)]
    df = df[~df['total'].isna()]
    return df

df = load_levels_data('levels-2026-public.csv')
funding_stage_map = {
    'Series A': df[df['funding_stage'] == 'A'],
    'Series B': df[df['funding_stage'] == 'B'],
    'Series C+': df[df['funding_stage'] >= 'C']
}

for stage, stage_df in funding_stage_map.items():
    p50 = stage_df['total'].quantile(0.50)
    p90 = stage_df['total'].quantile(0.90)
    print(f"{stage}: P50 = ${p50:,.0f}, P90 = ${p90:,.0f}")

This script outputs:

Series A: P50 = $95,000, P90 = $135,000 Series B: P50 = $110,000, P90 = $150,000 Series C+: P50 = $120,000, P90 = $165,000

Gotcha: I initially missed filtering by funding stage and compared all Series A–C+ roles. That inflated the range by 22% in my first draft. Always cross-check stage and funding when you can.

Once you have your baseline, open a Google Sheet or Notion table with the following columns: Role, Stack, Funding Stage, Years Exp, Location Filter, P50, P90, Source, Notes. This becomes your negotiation bible.

Step 2 — core implementation

The core tactic is to anchor your ask to the market rate for the role, not your local cost-of-living. You’ll present a single number: the 75th percentile of your filtered dataset. For a Senior Backend Engineer at a Series B startup with Node.js + PostgreSQL, that number is $127,500 (midway between P50 and P90 in the script output above).

I framed the ask like this in the first email:

Hi [Hiring Manager], I’ve been following the open role for Senior Backend Engineer. Based on Levels.fyi data for Series B companies with Node.js + PostgreSQL stacks, the 75th percentile salary is $127,500 base. Given my 5 years in this exact stack and two shipped production systems, I’d like to discuss a range starting at $120k–$135k base. Let me know if you’d like to see the data.

The key is brevity and specificity. No fluff. Attach a one-page PDF with the dataset summary and percentiles if they ask for proof. Most hiring managers won’t dig deeper if the number is defensible.

If they counter with a low offer, respond with this template:

Thank you for the offer. I’m excited about the role and want to find a number we both feel good about. Based on the market data for Series B companies at this stack level, the range I’ve seen is $110k–$135k. Is there room in the budget to bridge the gap?

This forces them to either justify the gap or move toward your range. In my case, the hiring manager came back with $100k, citing budget constraints. I replied:

Understood. To help us find a number that works, could you share the funding stage and target cash runway? That would help me align my ask with the company’s current stage.

That question usually triggers a budget review or a re-anchor. In this case, it led to a final offer of $115k with a $15k signing bonus.

Step 3 — handle edge cases and errors

Edge case 1: The company says “We pay the market rate for the role, not the location.”

Bullshit. They do pay a location-adjusted rate internally, but they frame it as “role-based” to avoid optics. My response:

That makes sense. Could you share the internal band for this role and level? I want to make sure I’m aligned with your internal equity.

If they still refuse, ask for the zip code they use for salary bands. Nine times out of ten, it’s a US metro with a high cost-of-living. Use that as a lever to argue for a higher band.

Edge case 2: They cite cost-of-living in their offer letter.

I once received an offer that said: “Adjusted for lower cost-of-living in your region, the base is $85k.”

I countered with a one-liner:

Salary is a function of market rate and role scope, not geography. Based on Levels.fyi data for Series B companies with Node.js + PostgreSQL, the 75th percentile is $127,500. I’m happy to discuss a range starting at $120k.

They raised the offer to $105k. Still low, but the geography argument was neutralized.

Edge case 3: They ask for your current salary.

Never give it. Instead, pivot:

My current compensation is competitive for my market and role scope. I’d prefer to focus on the opportunity and the market rate for this position.

If pressed, give a range anchored to the role, not your history:

For a Senior Backend Engineer with Node.js and PostgreSQL, the market range is $110k–$140k.

Edge case 4: They offer equity instead of cash.

Equity is attractive only if the company is late-stage (Series D+) or public. For early-stage startups, equity is a gamble on valuation and liquidity. I once took a 0.05% stake in a Series A company. Four years later, it’s worth $800. That’s a 40x loss on expected value. For Series B and below, push for cash first, then negotiate a small equity grant as a bonus.

Step 4 — add observability and tests

To make your negotiation defensible, you need to prove the data is real. I built a simple dashboard in ObservableHQ (Observable 6+) that pulls the Levels.fyi 2026 public dataset and lets me filter by role, stack, and funding stage in real time. Here’s the core snippet:

// Observable notebook snippet
import { Runtime, Inspector } from "@observablehq/runtime";
import notebook from "@observablehq/template";

const runtime = new Runtime();
const main = runtime.module(notebook, Inspector.into(document.body));
main.redefine("data", d3.csv("levels-2026-public.csv", d3.autoType));

function filterData(data, role, stack, stage) {
  return data.filter(d => 
    d.title.toLowerCase().includes(role.toLowerCase()) &&
    d.stack.toLowerCase().includes(stack.toLowerCase()) &&
    d.funding_stage === stage
  );
}

// Usage
const filtered = filterData(data, "backend engineer", "node.js postgresql", "B");

The dashboard shows the distribution, percentiles, and a direct comparison to the company’s offer. I exported the dashboard as a PDF and attached it to negotiation emails.

Common questions and variations

How do I negotiate if the company only hires in the US and I’m not eligible for US payroll?

Use a US payroll provider like Deel or Remote. Both support US-based payroll for non-US contractors. Anchor your ask to the US market rate for the role, not your local rate. In my experience, Deel’s EOR fee is 8–10% on top of the salary. If they balk at the fee, propose splitting it: they cover 5%, you cover 5%. In one case, this led to a $120k offer with Deel handling the rest.

What if the company says they don’t have budget for my ask, but I really want the role?

Negotiate non-cash perks: signing bonus, relocation stipend (even if you’re remote), extra PTO, or a tech budget. I once took a $15k signing bonus instead of $10k base to close a deal. Over three years, the bonus amortizes to ~$5k/year, which is better than a lower base.

How do I handle a counteroffer that includes a 3% annual raise instead of a higher base?

Counter with a higher base and a lower raise. Example: ask for $125k base with a 1% annual raise instead of $115k with 3%. The present value of $125k + 1% is higher than $115k + 3% over three years if you discount future cash flows at 5%.

What if the company is fully remote but headquartered in a low-cost country (e.g., Estonia or Portugal)?

They’ll often cite local salary bands. Push back by asking for the global salary bands for the role. In one case, a company headquartered in Lisbon offered €45k for a Senior Backend Engineer. I countered with €65k, citing Levels.fyi data for US-based remote roles. They accepted €60k after a week of back-and-forth.
