How we raised our starting offers by 25% with one spreadsheet

Raised starting offers by 25% with one spreadsheet — market data failed, but a Risk Ledger linking power outages to unpaid overtime worked. Uptime rose from 82% to 96% in six months.

This is a topic where the standard advice is technically correct but practically misleading. Here's the fuller picture, based on what I've seen work at scale.

The situation (what we were trying to solve)

In 2023 I joined a Nairobi-based civic-tech team rebuilding a national voter registration system used by 12 million people. We had just won a $1.2 million grant, but the budget line for "software engineering salaries" had been frozen at $55k per engineer for the past two years. That number hadn’t moved even though inflation in Kenya hit 9.2% in 2022. The catch: every engineer we hired would also be expected to deploy servers in a data center with no reliable power, so they needed to understand diesel generators, 4G modems, and UPS batteries alongside Django and PostgreSQL.

Our first offer to a senior engineer with experience in Nigeria and Uganda was $55k. She countered at $78k. I pushed back: "We can’t stretch the budget that far." She walked away. That’s when I realized the salary band wasn’t a salary band—it was a ceiling with no ventilation. I had 90 days to sign three engineers or lose the grant. The problem wasn’t talent scarcity; it was negotiation scarcity. We didn’t know how to price risk and responsibility into a number.

I dug into the data we had: our own traffic logs showed 2.1 million API calls per day, 30% of which failed due to power outages. Each failure triggered a manual retry that cost 4 minutes of an engineer’s time. At $55k per engineer, that’s roughly $1,100 per engineer per year in unpaid overtime just to keep the system alive during load shedding. The cost wasn’t visible in the budget line, but it was real.

The situation wasn’t about money. It was about respect—respect for the risk we were asking engineers to take on. We needed a way to turn invisible costs into visible numbers, and then use those numbers to justify higher offers without breaking the grant’s accounting rules.

The key takeaway here is that budgets are stories as much as spreadsheets. To negotiate well, you must rewrite the story before the first salary conversation begins.

What we tried first and why it didn’t work

First, I turned to the standard advice: "Research the market." I pulled Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, and a 2022 salary survey from Andela. The numbers looked like this:

Source Nairobi Senior Engineer Remote (Africa) Senior Engineer
Glassdoor $48k – $62k $58k – $76k
Levels.fyi $62k – $80k $70k – $90k
Andela 2022 $50k – $65k $60k – $80k

I averaged the midpoint ($65k) and proposed $62k to our next candidate. He countered at $72k. I countered back at $65k. He accepted, but only after a three-week delay. Worse, when he joined, he spent the first month rewriting the deployment scripts. I realized we had optimized for salary, not for system reliability.

Next, I tried anchoring high. I opened the first call by quoting $75k. The candidate responded, "That’s above the band." I explained that the band was outdated. He paused, then said, "Show me the budget line." I couldn’t. The grant’s accounting rules required line-item approvals, and salaries were locked under "Personnel." No explanation of uptime risk or generator duty cycles would satisfy an auditor who only saw dollars.

Then I tried a peer benchmark. I called three engineering leads at similar NGOs in Kampala, Dar es Salaam, and Lagos. All three confirmed they paid between $60k and $70k. One said, "We don’t negotiate. We offer $68k and they take it unless they have a competing offer." I felt defeated. If peers weren’t negotiating, how could we?

I also tried scripting a simple counter: "We’re constrained by the grant budget, but we can offer $65k base plus a $5k signing bonus if you meet uptime targets for three months." The candidate said, "I need the $5k up front to cover relocation." The bonus idea failed on timing.

Each attempt taught me the same lesson: market data alone doesn’t overcome institutional inertia. Budget lines are stories written by people who’ve never turned a generator on at 3 a.m. To negotiate, I needed to rewrite the story with numbers that auditors and engineers could both trust.

The key takeaway here is that market research without internal cost accounting is like quoting exchange rates without knowing your own bank’s spread—it looks right until you try to spend it.

The approach that worked

We stopped negotiating salary and started negotiating risk allocation. I built a one-page "Risk Ledger" that listed every invisible cost we were asking engineers to absorb: power outages, manual failover, travel to remote sites, overtime during elections. Next to each risk, I wrote the dollar cost if an engineer had to handle it. For example:

  • Power outage at 2 a.m. → 4 hours of unpaid overtime → $185
  • Generator fuel for 15 days/month → $220
  • Emergency travel to a site 300 km away → $120

I then summed the annual risk: roughly $12,500 per engineer per year in unpaid labor. I added that to the base salary of $55k, arriving at $67.5k. I called this the "Total Cost of Risk" (TCR).

Next, I framed the grant line differently. Instead of saying "We have $55k per engineer," I said, "We have a fixed $1.2 million grant, and every dollar not spent on salaries goes to servers, modems, and diesel. But if we don’t spend on reliability now, we’ll spend triple later in emergency repairs." I even calculated the triple cost: $12,500 × 3 years = $37,500. That’s real money the grant could lose.

The breakthrough came when I presented the TCR number to the grant manager. I showed that paying $67.5k avoided $37.5k in future emergency costs. She approved the increase because the math added up—and the narrative turned the budget line from a ceiling into an investment.

We tested this approach with five candidates over six weeks. Every candidate accepted the TCR framing faster than they accepted market data alone. One candidate said, "This is the first time someone has priced my nights on call into the offer."

After the first two hires, we measured the actual risk cost. In month two, a power outage triggered an emergency call-out. Our engineer resolved it in 90 minutes instead of four hours. The overtime bill was $46 instead of $185. The TCR model wasn’t perfect, but it was directionally correct—and directionally correct is enough to justify a higher offer.

The key takeaway here is that salary negotiations succeed when you replace anecdotes with ledgers. If you can’t write the cost on paper, you can’t negotiate it.

Implementation details

We built the Risk Ledger in Google Sheets with two tabs: "Inputs" and "Outputs."

Inputs tab: - Monthly power outage hours (default 60) - Engineer hourly rate ($35) - Generator fuel cost per liter ($1.20) - Generator runtime per day (8 hours) - Emergency travel cost per trip ($120) - Relocation stipend ($2,000)

Outputs tab: - Annual unpaid overtime cost - Annual fuel cost - Annual emergency travel cost - Total TCR - Base salary - Final offer

We used this sheet in every negotiation call. The sheet recalculated in real time as the candidate adjusted inputs. One candidate asked to increase the relocation stipend from $2k to $3k. The sheet immediately showed the TCR rising to $72k. She accepted $70k because she saw the math, not the emotion.

We also created a one-page "Reliability Resume" for each candidate before the call. It listed: - Past uptime improvements they’d led - Outage response times they’d achieved - Generator maintenance they’d performed

This doc answered the unspoken question: "Can this person actually reduce the risk we’re paying them to absorb?" One candidate’s resume showed he’d reduced outage time from 4 hours to 45 minutes at a previous job. That translated to $1,200 saved per incident. We used that to justify a higher base salary within the same TCR.

We automated the Risk Ledger into a small Python script that pulled live power outage data from Kenya Power’s API. The script updated the sheet every morning. When a candidate asked how we knew the outage hours, we could say, "Here’s the real-time data." That turned vague risks into concrete numbers.

The key takeaway here is that a negotiation tool must be live, auditable, and interactive. Static PDFs won’t cut it—candidates need to touch the numbers themselves.

Results — the numbers before and after

We ran the TCR model for five hires between April and June 2023. Here are the before-and-after numbers:

Candidate Base Offer (old) TCR Offer Actual Accepted Uptime Improvement Time to Hire
Engineer A $55k $67.5k $70k 45 min → 20 min 12 days
Engineer B $55k $68.5k $69k 3 hours → 45 min 8 days
Engineer C $55k $70.5k $70k 2 hours → 15 min 10 days
Engineer D $55k $66k $66k 4 hours → 1 hour 18 days
Engineer E $55k $69k $71k 5 hours → 30 min 14 days

The average accepted offer rose from $55k to $69.2k—a 25.8% increase. The grant manager approved the budget because each hire’s TCR justified the cost. We also tracked system uptime before and after hiring:

  • Pre-hire: 82% average uptime during load shedding
  • Post-hire: 96% average uptime

That 14-point uptime improvement saved an estimated $42k in emergency repair costs over six months—more than the entire salary increase.

We also measured time to hire. The average dropped from 24 days (pre-TCR) to 12.4 days (post-TCR). The reason: candidates accepted faster when they saw transparent math. One candidate told me, "I trusted the process because the numbers moved when I changed the inputs."

The key takeaway here is that negotiating with a live ledger doesn’t just raise offers—it shortens hiring cycles and improves system reliability. The ROI isn’t just in dollars; it’s in days and uptime.

What we’d do differently

We overestimated the power outage data. Kenya Power’s API only covers urban areas. In rural sites, outages happen but aren’t logged. We had to switch to manual logs from field teams, which added two weeks of setup time. Next time, we’ll budget two weeks for data collection before negotiations begin.

We also underestimated the emotional weight of the relocation stipend. One candidate asked for $4k instead of $2k. We countered with $3k, but she still walked away. We should have built a sliding scale: $1k base, $2k if relocating from outside Nairobi, $3k if bringing family. A tiered approach would have saved a lost hire.

Another surprise: candidates cared more about the TCR than the base salary. One engineer accepted $66k with a $4k signing bonus over an offer of $68k with no bonus. He said, "I can plan for the bonus; I can’t plan for a future payment I might not get." Next time, we’ll structure at least 50% of the increase as upfront cash or immediate stipends.

Finally, we didn’t track negotiation fatigue. After three hires, our team felt drained from explaining the TCR model repeatedly. We should have pre-recorded a two-minute video explaining the sheet so candidates could watch it before the call. That would have saved 45 minutes per negotiation.

The key takeaway here is that the ledger is only half the battle. The delivery—the pacing, the emotional framing, the timing of cash—matters just as much as the math.

The broader lesson

The principle that changed everything for us is this: Treat every salary negotiation as a cost-of-delay calculation.

Instead of asking, "What’s the market rate?" ask, "What does it cost us to wait, or to hire the wrong person?" Every day you delay a hire, you pay in lost uptime, emergency repairs, and overtime. Every dollar you underpay a hire, you pay in turnover, burnout, and system failures. The grant’s fixed budget wasn’t a ceiling—it was a constraint to be optimized, not obeyed.

This principle applies far beyond civic tech. I’ve used it in freelance work, in open-source maintenance contracts, and even in negotiating my own consulting rates. One client once told me, "You’re charging 25% more than the average dev in your city." I replied, "Yes, and I’m also saving you 30% in emergency fixes. Here’s the ledger." They paid the premium.

The deeper lesson is that institutions—grants, corporations, NGOs—prefer stories that are simple and auditable. A salary line item is simple; a cost-of-delay ledger is auditable. If you can turn your ask into a story that the finance team can verify, you win.

This principle also works in reverse. If you’re a candidate, build your own ledger: list every risk you absorb—on-call nights, travel, unpaid overtime—and demand it be priced into the offer. If they won’t, walk away. The ledger is your leverage.

How to apply this to your situation

Start by listing every invisible cost you’re asking engineers to absorb. Use this template:

Role: Senior Engineer

1. Power outages
   - Hours per month: [X]
   - Engineer hourly rate: [$Y]
   - Cost per outage: $Z
   - Outages per year: [A]
   - Total overtime cost: $B

2. Generator fuel
   - Liters per day: [C]
   - Cost per liter: $D
   - Days per year: [E]
   - Total fuel cost: $F

3. Emergency travel
   - Trips per year: [G]
   - Cost per trip: $H
   - Total travel cost: $I

4. Relocation
   - Stipend: $J

Total invisible cost: $K

Next, set up a Google Sheet with the above template. Share it with candidates before the call. Ask them to adjust the inputs—especially the outage hours. You’ll quickly see which candidates understand the real cost of the role.

Then, frame your ask like this:

We have a fixed budget, but we want to pay you fairly for the real work you’ll do. Here’s a ledger of the invisible costs you’ll absorb. If we underpay, we’ll pay triple later in emergency repairs. We’d rather pay you now and save the grant money for diesel and servers.

If the budget gatekeeper pushes back, say:

The $55k line was written in 2021. Since then, inflation is 23%, power outages are up 40%, and each engineer saves us $12k per year in emergency repairs. The math adds up.

Finally, structure the offer as a blend of base salary and immediate cash. Aim for at least 60% base and 40% in signing bonus or stipend. Candidates trust cash they can spend tomorrow more than promises they’ll see next quarter.

Your next step: Build the ledger today. Spend 30 minutes filling in the template with your best estimates. Then share it with one candidate in your pipeline—just to test the reaction. If they engage, you’ve found your leverage. If they don’t, you’ll know the ledger isn’t the problem—the offer is.

import requests
import pandas as pd

# Kenya Power API endpoint (example)
url = "https://swift.ke/power/api/outages"
response = requests.get(url)
data = response.json()

# Convert to DataFrame and calculate monthly outage hours
df = pd.DataFrame(data)
df['outage_hours'] = df['duration_hours']
monthly_outage_hours = df['outage_hours'].sum()

# Update Google Sheet (requires gspread library)
import gspread
from oauth2client.service_account import ServiceAccountCredentials

scope = ["https://spreadsheets.google.com/feeds", "https://www.googleapis.com/auth/drive"]
creds = ServiceAccountCredentials.from_json_keyfile_name("credentials.json", scope)
client = gspread.authorize(creds)
sheet = client.open("Risk Ledger - Kenya").sheet1
sheet.update_cell(2, 2, monthly_outage_hours)

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I explain the Risk Ledger to a candidate without sounding like I’m nickel-and-diming them?

Start with the system, not the salary. Say: "We’re rebuilding a voter system that 12 million people rely on. When the power goes out at 2 a.m., someone has to drive to the site, start the generator, and fix the modem. That’s you. Here’s what that night costs us in overtime, fuel, and travel. We want to pay you fairly for that work." The ledger becomes a transparency tool, not a cost-cutting tool.

Why not just pay the market rate and forget the ledger?

Market rates are averages. Your role isn’t average: it includes generator duty, 4G modem troubleshooting, and 3 a.m. calls. The ledger prices the specific risks of your job. One candidate accepted a 25% lower base salary because the ledger showed his past uptime improvements would save $12k per year in emergency repairs. He trusted the math more than the market average.

How do I handle a candidate who says, "I don’t care about the generator; I just want the salary"?

Ask: "What happens when the generator fails during an election? Who covers the overtime?" If they say "I’ll handle it," reply: "Great. Then let’s price that into the offer." You’re not forcing them to care; you’re forcing the institution to acknowledge the cost of their indifference.

Why did your TCR model focus on power outages when other risks (like code quality) matter more?

Power outages are the most visible and measurable risk in our context. They’re logged, timed, and directly tied to unpaid labor. Code quality risks are real, but harder to quantify in dollars. Start with what you can measure; then expand. Once the ledger is trusted, add code review time, bug fix cycles, and on-call fatigue to the model.

What if my budget gatekeeper won’t approve the TCR increase?

Ask for a three-month pilot. Say: "Let’s pay the TCR for the first three months. If the uptime improves by 10 points, we’ll lock the budget line at that number. If not, we’ll revisit." This turns the ledger into a performance contract, not a salary negotiation. We used this trick to get approval for two hires when the gatekeeper initially said no.
