Why promotions take forever — and how to force the issue

Cut promotion wait time from 18 months to 90 days with a promotion packet—reverse-engineered rubrics, concrete artifacts, and real case studies from Brazil, Colombia, and Mexico. Learn the exact artifacts reviewers expect and how to pre-sell them before the cycle opens.

The one-paragraph version (read this first)

If you’ve been stuck at the same level for a year or more, it’s usually because your manager doesn’t see the gap between your current work and the expectations of the next level. The fastest way to close that gap is to publish a 90-day promotion packet that shows you’ve delivered the exact artifacts the next level requires, even if the company never told you those artifacts existed. I built my first production-grade feature in 2019 and still didn’t get promoted until I sat down and reverse-engineered the internal “leveling rubric” from scraps of feedback and job descriptions. After I started shipping promotion packets every quarter, my promotion rate went from 0 % to 100 % in 18 months. The method works for ICs and managers alike, but only if you treat the packet like a product spec: define the success criteria, build the deliverables, and pre-sell the reviewers before the review cycle opens.

Why this concept confuses people

Most promotion guides assume the company has a clear rubric. In my experience, that’s almost never true. At my first job in Medellín, the leveling document for “Senior Engineer” was a 1998 Word file labeled “Senior v2.doc” that hadn’t been updated since 2017. The company was acquired, the document disappeared, and suddenly the bar for “Senior” was whatever the current CTO muttered in hallway conversations. I spent six months optimizing our Kafka pipeline to 20 ms latency, only to be told during the promotion panel that I hadn’t shown enough “mentorship.” I later learned that the panel expected a 10-slide mentorship deck plus signed “mentorship agreements” from three mentees—none of which were mentioned in the original job description.

Another trap is confusing “visibility” with “impact.” I once added a company-wide Grafana dashboard that saved 15 % on cloud costs. My manager praised me in Slack, but the promotion committee said the work was “too operational.” The real issue wasn’t the work; it was that the dashboard didn’t map to any leveling bullet. I only realized this after I reverse-engineered the rubric and saw that the “Senior” level explicitly asked for “system-level design artifacts,” not cost savings.

Finally, people assume promotions are meritocratic. In Latin America, where I’ve built teams in Colombia and Mexico, promotions are often political. A friend in São Paulo once got stuck at “Mid” for 18 months because her manager’s favorite IC was angling for the same slot. She broke the logjam by publishing a packet that explicitly addressed the unspoken criteria—“cross-team influence”—by running a lunch-and-learn series that reached 80 % of engineering. Within 90 days she was promoted, and the favorite IC quietly moved on to another team.

The mental model that makes it click

Think of a promotion like a product launch. You have three stakeholders: your manager, the promotion committee, and yourself. Your manager is the closest to the product (you), so they can amplify or mute your signal. The committee is the market that decides whether to adopt your product (promotion to the next level). The gap between your current level and the next is the feature gap: you need to build the missing features before the launch window (promotion cycle) closes.

The key insight is that the rubric is rarely written down, but the artifacts the rubric requires are usually scattered across performance reviews, skip-level notes, and hallway conversations. I collected 47 feedback snippets over 14 months at one company and mapped each to a leveling bullet. The pattern that emerged was shockingly simple: every level required three artifacts—one written, one delivered, one socialized. For “Senior,” the written artifact was a system-design doc, the delivered artifact was a cross-team feature, and the socialized artifact was a lunch-and-learn. Once I had that map, I could plan my work in 90-day chunks instead of waiting for the annual cycle.

Another way to see it is through the lens of “career debt.” If you don’t proactively close the gap, the debt compounds. I once skipped mentorship artifacts for a year because I assumed my code reviews counted. When the promotion panel asked for “evidence of scalable mentorship,” I had nothing. I had to do a 6-month catch-up that delayed my promotion by a full cycle. Treating the gap as debt forces you to allocate time every quarter for the missing artifacts, not just for the features your manager assigned.

A concrete worked example

Let’s use a real promotion packet I built for “Staff Engineer” at a marketplace startup in Mexico City. The goal was to close the gap between “Senior” and “Staff” in one 90-day cycle. The company had no written rubric, but we reverse-engineered it from the VP’s public slides and the CTO’s internal memo. The map looked like this:

Level Written artifact Delivered artifact Socialized artifact
Senior System-design doc Cross-team feature Lunch-and-learn
Staff Multi-system RFC Org-wide platform Engineering blog + panel participation

I already had the Senior artifacts from my previous cycle, so the gap was the Staff line. I set a 90-day goal: ship a multi-system RFC, launch an org-wide platform, and publish an engineering blog plus a panel talk.

Step 1: RFC I wrote a 15-page RFC titled “Unified Cart & Inventory API: Reducing 200 ms p99 latency.” The doc included context, trade-offs, rollout plan, and cost. I circulated it to five staff engineers and the VP of Platform, collected 23 comments, and published a v2. By day 15, I had explicit buy-in from three staff engineers and the VPE—pre-sales done.

Step 2: Platform I built a feature toggle service in Go that wrapped the Cart and Inventory APIs. I used Go 1.21, Gin 1.9, and Redis 7.0 as the toggle store. The service reduced Cart API p99 latency from 200 ms to 45 ms and cut cloud spend by $12 k/month. I shipped it behind a feature flag, ran it in prod for 14 days with 0 incidents, and wrote a post-mortem that the CTO later quoted in an all-hands.

Step 3: Blog + Panel I published an engineering blog titled “How we cut Cart latency by 77 % without rewriting the monolith.” It got 2.3 k reads in 48 hours and was retweeted by two engineering VPs outside the company. I also submitted a talk to the internal tech conference and was accepted to a panel on “Scaling Latin American marketplaces.”

By day 90, I bundled the RFC, the platform launch notes, the blog post, and the panel recording into a single PDF titled “Staff Promotion Packet Q3 2023.” I sent it to my manager, the VPE, and the CTO with a 3-line email: “Here’s the packet. I’m ready for the Staff panel whenever you schedule it.” Three weeks later, the panel approved the promotion. The entire process took 90 days and cost $0 in external tools beyond the infra I already had.

How this connects to things you already know

If you’ve ever launched a feature, you already know how to run a promotion packet. A feature spec is a mini-rubric: you define success criteria, build the deliverables, and pre-sell the reviewers (PM, EM, stakeholders). The only difference is the scope: instead of a feature spec, the rubric is the leveling rubric. The workflow is identical—research, outline, draft, review, ship, retrospect.

Another overlap is incident response. When an incident happens, you write a post-mortem, socialize it in a blameless post-mortem, and update runbooks. That’s three artifacts: written, delivered, socialized. A promotion packet is just an incident response for your career: you write the packet (post-mortem), deliver the artifacts (feature/mentorship/platform), and socialize via lunch-and-learn/blog/panel (runbook update).

Even the tools overlap. I used the same Git repo for my Staff packet that I used for RFCs: a docs folder with monthly subfolders. Each subfolder contained the artifact in markdown, the PR link, and the reviewer comments. When review time came, I exported the repo to PDF with pandoc—same toolchain I use for RFCs. Consistency lowers cognitive load and reduces the chance of missing an artifact.

Common misconceptions, corrected

Misconception 1: “If I just do great work, the promotion will happen.” I tracked 12 peers who consistently delivered high-impact work but were stuck at the same level for 18–24 months. When I reverse-engineered their packets, every single one had missing artifacts. One peer had shipped three cross-team features but hadn’t written a single system-design doc—so the panel couldn’t see the “system-level thinking” they expected. Another had mentored five engineers but hadn’t published a mentorship agreement or a lunch-and-learn—so the panel couldn’t verify the “scalable mentorship” bullet. Great work is necessary but not sufficient; you must also package it as the artifacts the rubric expects.

Misconception 2: “The rubric is fixed and written down somewhere.” At one company, the “Senior” rubric was a Google Doc titled “Expectations v3.2” that hadn’t been updated since 2020. Meanwhile, the CTO quietly changed the bar in skip-levels: suddenly “mentorship” meant “published internal blogs,” not just “code reviews.” The written rubric was outdated, but the skip-level feedback was the real signal. Treat the written rubric as a starting point, not the final word.

Misconception 3: “I can’t start until I know the exact rubric.” I waited six months at a startup because I couldn’t find the rubric. By then, I had missed two promotion cycles. Once I started collecting scraps—feedback snippets, job descriptions, CTO slides—I had 80 % of the rubric in 48 hours. The missing 20 % usually fills itself in during the drafting phase when reviewers ask for clarifications. Start with the scraps; you don’t need the full rubric to begin.

Misconception 4: “A promotion packet is bragging.” I once sent a packet that read like a resume: “I did X, I did Y, I did Z.” The panel rejected it because it lacked self-assessment. The fix was to frame each artifact as a solution to a problem stated in the rubric. For example, instead of “I wrote an RFC,” it became “I wrote an RFC to align Cart and Inventory APIs, reducing p99 latency from 200 ms to 45 ms.” The difference is subtle but crucial: the packet becomes a portfolio of solutions, not a list of achievements.

The advanced version (once the basics are solid)

Once you’ve shipped a few packets and know the pattern, you can optimize for velocity and scale. I call this the “packet factory.” The idea is to reuse artifacts across levels and teams so you’re not rebuilding the same doc every cycle.

Tooling I built a simple CLI in Python that generates a packet template from a YAML spec. The spec defines the level, the artifacts, and the reviewers. The CLI creates a folder structure, populates the markdown templates, and even inserts boilerplate sections like “Impact,” “Metrics,” and “Risks.” Example spec:

level: Staff
artifacts:
  - type: rfc
    title: Unified Cart & Inventory API
    reviewers: [staff1, staff2, vpe]
  - type: platform
    title: Feature toggle service
    metrics: [latency, cost]
  - type: social
    title: Engineering blog post
    channel: internal-blog

Running packet new --spec staff.yaml gives me a pre-structured folder with placeholders. I fill in the blanks, run packet export, and the PDF is ready.

Social proof The fastest way to pre-sell reviewers is to get early buy-in from staff+ engineers outside your reporting chain. I once had a manager who was skeptical about my Staff packet. I circulated the RFC to three staff engineers and the VPE, and when the panel asked about “cross-team influence,” I could point to their explicit approvals. Social proof short-circuits politics and reduces the chance of last-minute objections.

Cycles vs. ad-hoc Some companies have rigid promotion cycles; others are ad-hoc. I treat cycles as product sprints: if the cycle opens in Q3, I ship the packet in Q2 so reviewers have 30 days to digest it. For ad-hoc companies, I ship a rolling packet every 90 days and update it with new artifacts. The rolling packet becomes a living document that I can submit at any time.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I find the promotion rubric if it doesn’t exist?

Start with the job description for the next level. Highlight every bullet that isn’t already in your current packet. Then collect scraps: feedback snippets from 1:1s, skip-level notes, CTO slide decks, and internal memos. Look for patterns—recurring phrases like “system-level thinking” or “cross-team influence.” In one company, the phrase “published artifacts” appeared in 8 skip-levels but never in the job description. That became a clear signal for the next level.

What if my manager doesn’t support me?

If your manager is a blocker, pre-sell upward. Circulate your artifacts to staff+ engineers and VPs outside your chain. At one company, my manager was lukewarm, but the VPE explicitly praised my RFC in an all-hands. When the panel asked about “cross-team influence,” the VPE’s public endorsement carried more weight than my manager’s private reservations. Politics are inevitable; pre-selling reduces their impact.

Can I reuse artifacts from previous cycles?

Yes, but only if they map to the new rubric. I reused a lunch-and-learn from my Senior packet for my Staff packet because the rubric explicitly asked for “published internal talks.” The key is to re-frame the artifact to match the new level’s wording. For example, a “feature launch” from the Mid level can become a “platform launch” at the Senior level by adding metrics and rollback plans.

What if I miss the promotion cycle?

Treat it as a data point, not a failure. After I missed a cycle in 2022, I spent 30 days reverse-engineering why. The packet lacked a “socialized artifact”—a lunch-and-learn or blog post—so the panel couldn’t verify the “mentorship” bullet. Next cycle, I added the lunch-and-learn and got promoted. Missing a cycle is a gift: it reveals the exact gap you need to close.

The key takeaway here is

Promotions aren’t given; they’re negotiated with artifacts. You close the gap by shipping the exact deliverables the next level expects—even if the company never wrote those expectations down. Build a packet every 90 days, pre-sell reviewers, and treat the process like a product launch. The fastest engineers I know treat promotions as a feature they’re shipping to their managers and committees.

Next step

Open your notes app and list every bullet in your current level’s rubric. For each bullet you haven’t shipped as an artifact, schedule a 30-minute block this week to write the placeholder. If the rubric doesn’t exist, spend 60 minutes collecting scraps from feedback, job descriptions, and slide decks. By next Friday, you’ll have the skeleton of your first packet—and a clear path to the next level.
