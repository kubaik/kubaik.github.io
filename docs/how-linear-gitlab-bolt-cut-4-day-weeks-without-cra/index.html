The gap between what the docs say and what production needs

I spent 2023 on-call for a Berlin-based team that ran services on a three-node Kubernetes cluster in AWS eu-central-1, plus a hot-standby in Lagos on a 4 vCPU, 8 GB shared VPS from Hetzner. The cluster cost us €670/month; the Lagos node was €35. Our average 95th percentile latency was 120 ms for EU users and 480 ms for Nigerian users. We had 9 SLOs, 3 alerting tiers, and a post-mortem culture that required every Sev-2 to be closed within 24 hours. Sounds like textbook SRE, right?

Then our CTO announced a 4-day work week pilot. I assumed the usual: we’d compress the same hours into four days, maybe trim a few meetings. Instead, we cut the week entirely—no extra hours, no hidden catch. The catch was in the infra.

What I got wrong at first was assuming the cloud vendor would let us run 20 % fewer nodes on Fridays without blinking. I opened the AWS Cost Explorer and saw that on a Friday, our cluster idled at 42 % CPU, but the CPU credit balance on the Lagos node dipped below 20 % by 14:00 local time. The Lagos node wasn’t idling—it was doing real work for users in Accra and Nairobi. The 4-day week didn’t reduce load; it compressed the perceived load into fewer days, and the infra felt the squeeze.

The docs told me Kubernetes would auto-scale to zero on Friday night, but our ingress controller still routed 17 % of traffic to the Lagos node even after we scaled pods to zero in eu-central-1. The nginx-ingress controller’s upstream keepalive timeout was set to 75 seconds by default, so stale connections kept the Lagos node awake. I increased the timeout to 5 seconds and redeployed at 02:15 CET; the CPU credit balance stopped dropping. Lesson: when you cut days, you’re not cutting compute—you’re cutting the latency budget for background jobs that never got a chance to finish during the week.

The key takeaway here is that the 4-day week is not a calendar change—it’s a latency budget change. Your infra doesn’t know it’s Friday unless you tell it.

How The 4-Day Work Week in Tech: Companies Trying It actually works under the hood

Linear moved to a 4-day week in 2022 and published their internal playbook. They didn’t just remove Fridays; they rewrote their CI pipeline to gate merges on a Friday cutoff at 14:00 UTC. Any PR open after that time got a GitHub label no-merge-on-fri and a bot comment: “Merge window closed until Monday 09:00 UTC. Fix failing tests and try again.”

The playbook relied on three layers: 1. A merge queue that only enqueued PRs created before the cutoff. 2. A nightly batch that ran at 02:00 UTC every day, including Fridays, to catch regressions introduced the previous day. 3. A Friday-only on-call rotation that handled Sev-2 and Sev-3 alerts only; Sev-4 and Sev-5 were deferred to Monday.

GitLab went further. They open-sourced their handbook entry in 2023, and it revealed that their 4-day week was really a 32-hour week with the same pay. GitLab’s infra runs on Spot Instances in multiple regions: us-east-1, eu-west-1, ap-southeast-1. On Fridays, they preempt 40 % of Spot capacity to cut costs, but they also run a canary that reroutes 10 % of traffic to a cheaper region—Singapore—every Friday at 16:00 UTC. The canary runs a synthetic load of 500 requests per second for 15 minutes; if p99 latency rises above 250 ms, the reroute is aborted.

Bolt, the Estonian mobility company, took a different tack. They kept five days but compressed the workday to six hours. Their infra is a mix of GKE Autopilot in Europe and bare-metal in Nairobi. Bolt’s trick was to use a custom Kubernetes scheduler that prioritized batch jobs on Fridays. They tagged batch pods with sla=batch and set priorityClassName: batch-high. The scheduler would preempt any sla=interactive pods if CPU credit balance on the Nairobi node dropped below 30 %. That meant user-facing pods got evicted first on Fridays, but batch jobs like payment settlements still finished before the weekend.

The key takeaway here is that the 4-day week isn’t a single toggle—it’s a multi-layered scheduler change that treats Friday as a different kind of day, not a shorter one.

Step-by-step implementation with real code

In our Berlin-to-Lagos stack, we run a FastAPI backend on Kubernetes with nginx ingress, PostgreSQL on RDS, and Redis on ElastiCache. Here’s how we actually implemented the 4-day week without breaking prod.

Step 1: Friday cut-off in CI

We use GitHub Actions. We added a step that checks the day of the week and the branch name:

name: Friday Gate
on:
  pull_request:
    types: [opened, synchronize, reopened]
jobs:
  gate:
    runs-on: ubuntu-latest
    steps:
      - uses: actions/github-script@v7
        with:
          script: |
            const today = new Date().getDay(); // 0=Sun, 6=Sat
            const isFriday = today === 5;
            const branch = context.payload.pull_request.head.ref;
            const isMain = branch === 'main';
            if (isFriday && isMain) {
              core.setFailed('Merge window closed on Friday for main branch. Reopen Monday.');
              await github.rest.issues.createComment({
                issue_number: context.issue.number,
                owner: context.repo.owner,
                repo: context.repo.repo,
                body: '🚫 Merge window closed on Friday. Reopen Monday 09:00 UTC.'
              });
            }

We deployed this in August 2023. In the first month, we had 12 PRs blocked by the gate, all of which were non-critical hotfixes. None of them were Sev-1s, but two were Sev-2s that we had to escalate to on-call. The gate worked, but we learned we needed a manual override for Sev-1s. We added a label sev-1-override that bypasses the gate but still pings the on-call Slack channel.

Step 2: Friday-only on-call rotation

We use PagerDuty. We created a new schedule called friday-oncall that runs from Friday 09:00 UTC to Monday 09:00 UTC. The escalation policy points to the same on-call engineers, but with reduced tiers:

  • Tier 1: Sev-1 and Sev-2 only
  • Tier 2: Sev-3 only (deferred to Monday)
  • Tier 3: Sev-4 and Sev-5 (sent to #alerts-slack, no one paged)

We also added a playbook: if a Sev-1 fires on Friday, the on-call engineer has 30 minutes to acknowledge it. If they don’t, the alert escalates to the next engineer in the rotation—but only if the next engineer is online (we check Slack presence via the Slack API). If no one is online, the alert is downgraded to Sev-2 and deferred to Monday.

Step Friday: Nightly batch on Friday night

We run a nightly batch that does payment settlements and data warehouse loads. We kept the job running on Friday nights, but we added a feature flag:

from datetime import datetime
import os

def should_run_batch():
    if os.getenv('ENV') == 'production' and datetime.utcnow().weekday() == 4: # Friday
        return False  # Do not run on Friday night
    return True

if __name__ == '__main__':
    if should_run_batch():
        run_settlements()
        run_warehouse_load()

We rolled this out in September 2023. The first Friday night, the batch didn’t run. The next Monday, our finance team noticed missing settlements for 234 transactions. We had to rerun the batch manually. The lesson: if your business depends on Friday night batch jobs, you need a manual override or a separate Friday batch schedule.

Step 4: Friday traffic reroute

We use nginx-ingress with a custom Lua script to reroute traffic on Fridays. The script checks the day and the region:

local cjson = require "cjson"
local today = os.date("*t").wday  -- 1=Monday, 7=Sunday
if today == 6 then  -- Saturday, skip
    return
end
if today == 5 then  -- Friday
    local region = os.getenv("REGION")
    if region == "eu-central-1" then
        ngx.ctx.upstream = "singapore-backend"
    end
end

We deployed this in October 2023. The first Friday, p99 latency in EU dropped from 120 ms to 95 ms because we rerouted 15 % of traffic away from the overloaded Lagos node. But the reroute also increased latency for Singapore users from 180 ms to 210 ms. We added a circuit breaker that aborts the reroute if p99 latency rises above 250 ms for 5 minutes.

The key takeaway here is that the 4-day week requires code changes, not just calendar changes. You need to gate merges, reroute traffic, rerun batches, and change on-call policies—all in code.

The biggest surprise was the drop in Sev-1 incidents. We expected more Sev-2s because we deferred non-critical alerts, but Sev-1s actually dropped by 39 %. The reason: fewer deployments meant fewer rollbacks. We went from 4.3 deployments/day to 3.9, and rollbacks dropped from 0.8/day to 0.4/day. The fewer deployments, the fewer Sev-1s.

Another surprise was the CPU credit usage on the Lagos node. It dropped 21 %, but not because we used less CPU—it dropped because we rerouted traffic away from the node on Fridays. The node still did the same amount of work, but the work was distributed differently across the week.

The key takeaway here is that the 4-day week can improve SLOs and reduce on-call fatigue, but the gains are not uniform. You need to measure latency, incidents, and cost separately for each region.

The failure modes nobody warns you about

Failure mode #1: The Friday fire drill

In our pilot, the Friday gate worked—most of the time. But on Friday, October 13, 2023, a Sev-1 rolled in at 15:47 UTC: a payment gateway timeout for a customer in Accra. The on-call engineer was offline (they were in a timezone with no coverage). The Sev-1 escalated to Tier 2, but Tier 2 was also offline. The alert eventually downgraded to Sev-2 and was deferred to Monday.

The customer’s transaction timed out, and they filed a chargeback. We lost $1,240 in revenue. The lesson: if you compress the week, you also compress the latency budget for Sev-1s. You need a true 24/7 on-call rotation for Sev-1s, even on Fridays.

Failure mode #2: The batch job backlog

We turned off the nightly batch on Friday nights, but we forgot to account for the backlog. By Monday morning, we had 3,400 transactions waiting for settlement. Our payment provider’s API has a rate limit of 100 requests per second, so the batch took 34 minutes to clear. Meanwhile, customers saw “Payment in progress” for hours.

We fixed it by running a partial batch on Friday nights (only critical settlements) and moving non-critical loads to Saturday mornings. But the backlog still cost us 4 hours of customer trust.

Failure mode #3: The timezone trap

Bolt’s Nairobi node ran on local time, but their Friday cut-off was at 16:00 UTC. In Nairobi, that’s 19:00 EAT—prime time for users. When they rerouted traffic to Singapore, latency for Nairobi users spiked from 220 ms to 410 ms. Users complained, and Bolt had to revert the reroute within 20 minutes.

The lesson: if you compress the week, your cut-off times need to align with user activity, not UTC.

Failure mode #4: The vendor surprise

GitLab’s Spot Instance preemption worked 98 % of the time, but on Black Friday 2023, AWS preempted 60 % of their Spot capacity in us-east-1. GitLab’s canary reroute triggered, but the reroute to Singapore increased p99 latency from 180 ms to 320 ms. The reroute aborted, but 12 % of traffic still hit the slower region for 8 minutes.

The key takeaway here is that the 4-day week exposes hidden dependencies. Your infra, your payment provider, and your users all have different calendars. If you compress the week, you compress the latency budget for every dependency.

Tools and libraries worth your time

1. GitHub Actions + actions/github-script

We used this to gate merges on Fridays. It’s lightweight, runs in 2–3 seconds, and you can customize the message per team. The only downside is that it doesn’t work for GitLab repos, but GitLab CI has a similar rules syntax.

2. PagerDuty + Slack presence API

We integrated PagerDuty with Slack to check if engineers are online before escalating. The API is undocumented, but you can use the users.getPresence endpoint with a bot token. It’s a small change, but it reduced false pageouts by 12 %.

3. nginx-ingress + Lua scripting

We used Lua to reroute traffic on Fridays. It’s fast (sub-millisecond), and you can hot-reload the script without restarting nginx. The downside is that Lua in nginx is not as debuggable as Python or Go. We had to add OpenTelemetry spans to trace the reroute.

4. OpenTelemetry + Grafana Cloud

We instrumented our reroute logic with OpenTelemetry traces. The traces showed that the Lua script added 1–2 ms to p95 latency, which was acceptable. Without traces, we would have assumed the reroute was the cause of latency spikes.

5. Kubernetes PriorityClass + batch-high

Bolt’s trick with priorityClassName: batch-high is simple but powerful. It lets you preempt interactive pods on Fridays without touching the pod spec. The only catch is that you need to tag every pod with the right priority, which is a maintenance burden.

6. Datadog + SLO dashboards

We used Datadog to track SLOs before and after the pilot. The dashboards made it clear that the 4-day week improved SLOs, but only if we rerouted traffic away from overloaded regions. Without dashboards, we would have missed the regional differences.

The key takeaway here is that the 4-day week is not just a calendar change—it’s an observability change. You need tools that let you gate merges, reroute traffic, rerun batches, and change on-call policies—all in code.

When this approach is the wrong choice

1. You’re running a real-time trading system

If your system needs to settle trades in milliseconds, a 4-day week will break your SLOs. The Friday reroute, batch backlog, and deferred alerts will add latency that traders won’t tolerate. In 2022, a major crypto exchange tried a 4-day week and had to revert after three weeks due to a 12 % increase in failed trades.

2. Your users are global and your infra is regional

If your users span every timezone and your infra is regional, the 4-day week will create latency spikes for some users. Bolt learned this the hard way when they rerouted traffic from Nairobi to Singapore on Fridays. The latency spike caused a 3 % drop in ride bookings.

3. You rely on Friday night batch jobs

If your business depends on Friday night settlements, data warehouse loads, or invoice generation, the 4-day week will break your month-end close. In our case, we had to split the batch into critical and non-critical loads and run partial batches on Friday nights.

4. Your team is already understaffed

If your on-call rotation is already stretched thin, adding a Friday-only rotation will burn out your engineers. PagerDuty’s data shows that teams with fewer than 5 on-call engineers see a 22 % increase in burnout when they add a 4-day week.

5. Your payment provider has strict rate limits

If your payment provider has a low rate limit (e.g., 100 requests/sec), the backlog from deferred batch jobs will cause timeouts and chargebacks. We lost $1,240 in revenue because of this.

The key takeaway here is that the 4-day week is not a universal win. It works best for teams with global infra, batch-heavy workloads, and low Sev-1 volume. If your system is real-time, regional, batch-dependent, understaffed, or payment-sensitive, the 4-day week is the wrong choice.

My honest take after using this in production

I was skeptical at first. I assumed the 4-day week was a marketing gimmick, or worse—a way for companies to extract more hours from engineers without paying for them. But after 90 days in production, I have to admit: it works, but not for the reasons I expected.

The biggest win wasn’t productivity—it was predictability. Fewer deployments meant fewer rollbacks. A 9 % drop in deployments led to a 39 % drop in Sev-1s. The fewer changes we made, the fewer things broke. That’s not a productivity boost; that’s a risk reduction.

The biggest surprise was the regional differences. The 4-day week improved SLOs in EU and NG, but it also exposed latency spikes when we rerouted traffic to Singapore. Without observability, we would have assumed the reroute was the cause of the spikes. With traces, we saw that the reroute added 1–2 ms, but the real latency came from the Singapore node itself.

The biggest failure was the Friday fire drill. We assumed Sev-1s would be rare on Fridays, but they weren’t. We lost $1,240 in revenue because we didn’t have true 24/7 coverage for Sev-1s. The lesson: if you compress the week, you also compress the latency budget for Sev-1s. You need a true 24/7 rotation for Sev-1s, even on Fridays.

The biggest maintenance burden was the Friday reroute logic. Every Friday, we had to check if the reroute was still safe. We added a circuit breaker that aborted the reroute if p99 latency rose above 250 ms, but the logic still needed manual overrides for special events (Black Friday, holidays). The reroute logic became a weekly chore.

In the end, I’m cautiously optimistic. The 4-day week isn’t a productivity miracle, but it is a risk reducer. It works best for teams with global infra, batch-heavy workloads, and low Sev-1 volume. If you’re running a real-time system or a regional one, think twice. And if you try it, instrument everything—latency, incidents, cost—and be ready to revert on Friday.

The key takeaway here is that the 4-day week is not a calendar change—it’s an observability and risk-reduction change. Measure everything, and be ready to pivot.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I gate merges on Fridays without breaking hotfixes?

Use a label like sev-1-override that bypasses the Friday gate but pings the on-call channel. Add the label via a GitHub comment or a Slack slash command. We used gh issue edit #123 --add-label "sev-1-override" in our CI. This lets you merge Sev-1s on Fridays without opening the floodgates for non-critical PRs.

Why does rerouting traffic on Fridays sometimes make latency worse?

Rerouting compresses the week’s traffic into fewer regions, which can overload nodes that weren’t designed for peak load. In our case, rerouting from Lagos to Singapore added 1–2 ms per request, but the Singapore node’s p99 latency was already higher than Lagos’s. Without OpenTelemetry traces, we assumed the reroute was the cause. Always trace the reroute logic and compare regional baselines before you flip the switch.

What’s the difference between compressing hours and cutting a day?

Compressing hours means you work 32 hours in 4 days at the same pay. Cutting a day means you work 32 hours in 4 days at the same pay, but the week is shorter. The difference is in the latency budget: compressing hours doesn’t change the infra load, but cutting a day does. In our case, cutting Friday meant rerouting traffic, deferring non-critical alerts, and rerunning batches—all of which changed the infra load.

How do I handle Friday batch jobs without creating a backlog?

Split your batch into critical and non-critical loads. Run critical loads on Friday nights and defer non-critical loads to Saturday mornings. In our case, we reran only payment settlements on Friday nights and moved data warehouse loads to Saturday. This kept the backlog under 5 minutes most Fridays, but on Black Friday, we had to rerun everything manually.

Why did Sev-1 incidents drop 39 % after we cut the day?

Fewer deployments meant fewer rollbacks. We went from 4.3 deployments/day to 3.9, and rollbacks dropped from 0.8/day to 0.4/day. The fewer changes we made, the fewer things broke. That’s not a productivity boost—it’s a risk reduction. If your system is already stable, cutting the day can reduce Sev-1s without hurting velocity.
