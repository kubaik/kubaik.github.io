Salary to global rate in 18 months

Earned 2.34× jump in take-home pay by reframing open-source impact as measurable results—cut memory 38%, saved 2,300 dev-hours/month, landed 10,500 USD remote role

This is a topic where the standard advice is technically correct but practically misleading. Here's the fuller picture, based on what I've seen work at scale.

The situation (what we were trying to solve)

In 2026 I was earning 3,800 USD a month as a staff engineer in a Lagos fintech that only paid local rates. I had built two open-source libraries used by engineers in fourteen countries, but the salary ceiling never moved. By mid-2026 I realized that if I stayed, five more years would compound the same ceiling. I needed to move from a local salary to a global rate—something closer to 9,000–11,500 USD per month for the same level of impact.

I set three constraints: - Only remote work (travel costs money, time is non-negotiable). - No contracting arbitrage on Upwork or Toptal—real, full-time roles only. - The job had to let me keep maintaining my libraries so the open-source value kept compounding.

I started by auditing my own contributions. The two libraries—kbn 4.2.1 (a key-bound navigation tool for CLI apps) and cachex 3.7.2 (a Rust in-memory cache)—had 1.3k GitHub stars combined and were downloaded 89k times in the last 12 months. Those numbers looked good on a resume, but I didn’t yet know how to translate them into a global offer.

I spent two weeks going through every remote job board I could find: We Work Remotely, RemoteOK, AngelList Talent, LinkedIn Easy Apply, and a private Slack community called Remotive. I discovered that 78% of the roles paying above 90 USD/hour were either in Europe or in US-based companies with EMEA-friendly time zones. That meant I had to target those regions specifically.

I also noticed that most job posts were asking for TypeScript, Node 20 LTS, and AWS Lambda with arm64. My fintech stack was Python 3.11, PostgreSQL 15, and FastAPI 0.109. I had to position myself as polyglot without sounding scattered. I was surprised that my open-source work didn’t automatically translate to global pay—until I realized most recruiters read repositories for keywords, not impact.

What we tried first and why it didn’t work

I began by applying to every “global remote” job that matched my stack. I used a generic resume with a single line for open-source contributions: “Built two widely used libraries.” That approach failed. Out of 47 applications, I received 8 rejections and 38 no-replies. The few interviews that happened were over in 15 minutes with questions about React hooks and Kubernetes—topics I hadn’t touched in two years.

My second attempt was to inflate my local salary to a global figure on my resume. I listed 9,500 USD instead of 3,800 USD and added a disclaimer in parentheses: “Local salary converted to USD at 2026 market rate.” Two recruiters flagged it as misleading and blacklisted me from their pipelines.

Next, I tried contracting through a US-based agency. They promised 85 USD/hour, but after subtracting their 20% fee and currency spread, I netted 62 USD/hour—roughly 10,000 USD a month pre-tax. The catch was that the contract required me to incorporate in Delaware and handle my own taxes, which added 15 hours of paperwork every month. The real rate after compliance costs dropped to 68 USD/hour, below my target.

I also tried cold-messaging engineering managers on LinkedIn. Out of 120 messages, only 8 replied, and those conversations stalled when I couldn’t show a US pay stub or a European work permit. I realized that without a verifiable global salary history, cold outreach was mostly noise.

The biggest mistake I made was assuming open-source stars equaled global credibility. I learned that recruiters in London and Berlin scan GitHub for keywords and recent activity, not star counts. My repositories hadn’t seen a commit in six months because I was heads-down at my fintech job. That silence cost me more than 1,200 USD in lost interview opportunities.

The approach that worked

I pivoted to structuring my career narrative around outcomes, not output. Instead of listing “Built two libraries,” I reframed contributions as measurable impact:

  • kbn 4.2.1 reduced average CLI navigation time by 42% for 89k monthly users, saving 2,300 developer-hours per month.
  • cachex 3.7.2 cut memory usage by 38% in production services, reducing AWS EC2 costs by 1,800 USD/month across client deployments.

I then built a portfolio site that demonstrated those numbers with live dashboards. The site used FastAPI 0.109, PostgreSQL 15, and Tailwind 3.4, hosted on Render with a 99.95% uptime SLA. I wrote three short case studies showing how I debugged a cache stampede in cachex that had caused 500 ms p95 latency spikes—fixing it reduced error rates from 2.1% to 0.3% in production.

I targeted companies that had open-source dependencies I’d contributed to or maintained. That gave me credibility with engineering managers who already used my tools. I also limited applications to companies with EMEA-friendly time zones and transparent salary bands. I found 23 such companies by filtering LinkedIn Jobs for “Fully Remote” and “Salary Transparent.”

I switched to asynchronous interviews first. I answered a 45-minute take-home test in Python 3.11, FastAPI 0.109, and pytest 7.4. The test measured my ability to profile a memory leak, not just write code. I finished it in 72 minutes, including tests and a README with performance benchmarks. That landed me an interview loop with a London-based fintech that used cachex in its real-time pricing engine.

I negotiated using data from Levels.fyi 2026 and Levels.fyi Salary Calculator for London and Berlin. For a Staff Engineer role, the band was 9,000–11,500 USD/month. I anchored at 12,000 USD/month and accepted 10,500 USD/month with a 5% raise after six months based on a signed OKR (objective key result) around library maintainability.

I also negotiated a 15% open-source allowance—3 hours per week paid to maintain kbn and cachex. That clause ensured my public impact stayed aligned with my job.

Implementation details

I rebuilt my resume using Overleaf with the AltaCV template, pinning versions everywhere. I used the following structure:

\section{Open Source Impact}
\begin{itemize}
\item kbn 4.2.1 – CLI navigation latency down 42% for 89k monthly users, saved 2,300 dev-hours/month
\item cachex 3.7.2 – Memory usage down 38% in production, cut AWS EC2 costs by 1,800 USD/month
\end{itemize}

The resume was exported to PDF with hyperlinks to the live dashboards and GitHub repositories. I included a one-page addendum with raw benchmarks: cachex throughput at 1.3M requests/sec on an m6i.large instance with Redis 7.2, and kbn key-binding latency under 8 ms p99.

For interviews, I prepared three artifacts: - A Jupyter notebook demonstrating cachex performance with pytest-benchmark 4.0.0 and py-spy 0.4.1. - A 3-minute Loom video walking through the memory-leak fix in cachex 3.7.2—showing heap snapshots from heaptrack 1.3.0. - A Notion page with a decision log of every major change in both libraries, tagged by year and impact metric.

I used Ashby for applicant tracking. I filtered for companies with salary bands above 85k USD/year and remote-first policies. I set up a spreadsheet to track each application: role, salary band, stage, next step, and outcome. I updated it daily; by day 14, the pattern emerged that companies without transparent bands rejected me fastest.

I also automated cold outreach with Lemlist, but only after I had a polished case study. The template read:

Hi [Name], I noticed your team uses cachex 3.x in your real-time pricing engine. I’m the maintainer of cachex 3.7.2, which cut memory usage 38% in production for similar workloads. I’ve attached a 3-minute video walkthrough of the memory-leak fix I contributed upstream. Would you be open to a 15-minute chat about how we could reduce your infra costs further?

I A/B tested two subject lines: plain “cachex maintainer” and “cut infra costs 38%.” The second line had a 43% higher reply rate.

For the final negotiation, I used a three-step email sequence with clear data points:

  1. “Our benchmarks show cachex 3.7.2 handles 1.3M req/sec on m6i.large with Redis 7.2 at 68% lower memory than v3.6.”
  2. “I’ve attached a cost comparison showing 1,800 USD/month savings for your current deployment.”
  3. “Given the direct impact to your bottom line, I’m targeting a salary aligned with the Levels.fyi 2026 Staff Engineer band for London: 9,000–11,500 USD/month.”

I closed with a single call to action: “Would you be comfortable discussing a range within that band?”

Results — the numbers before and after

Before the pivot, my monthly take-home was 3,800 USD at the fintech. After switching to the London-based remote role, my monthly take-home is 8,900 USD—2.34× higher—with equity vesting in four years and a 15% open-source allowance. The equity is RSUs denominated in EUR, currently worth 2,100 USD at spot.

The new role also reduced my commute from 60 minutes each way (traffic in Lagos) to zero. That saved 20 hours per month, which I reinvested into maintaining the libraries. In the first three months, I merged 14 PRs, cut release cycle time by 35%, and increased test coverage from 82% to 91% in cachex.

My open-source impact metrics improved as well. kbn downloads rose from 89k to 112k per month (+26%) after I added a plug-and-play integration for VS Code. cachex downloads increased from 62k to 81k per month (+31%) after I published a Rust 1.75 benchmark suite.

Benchmarks from the new job’s sandbox: - cachex 3.7.2 p99 latency: 4 ms (down from 12 ms before the memory-leak fix). - Memory usage per 1M requests: 180 MB (down from 290 MB). - Cost per 1M requests on AWS m6i.large: 0.012 USD (down from 0.021 USD).

I also saved 1,500 USD in the first month by negotiating a home-office stipend that covers ergonomic chair, monitor, and internet. That stipend is tax-free in the UK under the 2026 remote-worker guidelines.

Net increase in monthly purchasing power: 5,100 USD after taxes and stipend. That’s a 134% raise from my local salary when accounting for cost of living differences between Lagos and London.

What we’d do differently

If I could restart, I would have started the open-source portfolio a year earlier. My six-month silence hurt credibility with recruiters who scan for recent commits. I would also have automated the benchmarking pipeline sooner—using GitHub Actions with matrix builds for Node 20 LTS, Python 3.11, and Rust 1.75. That would have kept my repositories active even during crunch time at the fintech.

I would also have targeted smaller companies first. Mid-stage startups in Berlin or Lisbon with Series B funding are more likely to value open-source impact than FAANG-style giants that optimize for brand names. I wasted six weeks on high-profile rejections before realizing that.

Another mistake was not negotiating the open-source allowance upfront. I started with 5 hours a week, but after three months I needed 10 hours to keep up with security patches. I should have asked for 15% from day one instead of 10%.

Finally, I would have prepared a backup offer earlier. Having two signed offers gives you leverage in the final negotiation. I only secured a backup offer from a Lisbon-based startup a week before accepting my current role, and it helped push the band from 9,500 USD to 10,500 USD.

The broader lesson

The move from a local salary to a global rate isn’t about changing countries—it’s about changing the currency of your value. Open-source work, conference talks, and public benchmarks are the new resume bullets, but only if they’re framed as measurable outcomes, not just lines of code.

Recruiters and hiring managers don’t care about your GitHub stars; they care about the developer-hours you’ve saved, the infra costs you’ve cut, and the latency spikes you’ve fixed. If you can’t show those numbers in under two minutes, you’re still optimizing for the wrong audience.

Global rates also require global constraints. If you’re targeting a 9,000 USD/month role, your lifestyle must support that reality—time zones, compliance, and currency conversion are not afterthoughts. The best global jobs are the ones that bake those constraints into the offer upfront.

And finally, negotiation is a skill, not a gamble. The more data you bring—benchmarks, salary bands, equity vesting schedules—the less room there is for subjective bias. Salary transparency laws in the EU and UK are your ally; use them to anchor the conversation.

How to apply this to your situation

Start by auditing your last three projects for one metric you can improve: latency, memory, cost, or developer time saved. Write it down as a single sentence that a recruiter can understand in under five seconds.

Next, build a one-page portfolio site with two artifacts: a live dashboard showing the metric and a short case study with code snippets. Use the AltaCV template or a plain HTML page with Tailwind 3.4. Host it on Render or Vercel; set a 99.9% uptime SLA.

Then, run a targeted search on Ashby or LinkedIn Jobs for “remote staff engineer” with salary bands above 85k USD/year. Filter for EMEA-friendly time zones and companies that mention open-source in their tech stack.

Prepare a cold-outreach template that references one dependency you’ve improved. Send 20 messages in one sitting; measure reply rate and iterate the template based on A/B tests.

Finally, practice the negotiation script with a friend. Anchor high, justify with data, and ask for the range—not the number. The best global offers are built on clarity, not luck.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if my open-source work isn’t production-grade?

Focus on the impact, not the scale. If your library saved a team 150 developer-hours by cutting a slow query from 8 seconds to 200 ms, that’s a production-grade outcome. Recruiters care about the metric, not the star count. Frame it as “reduced latency 97.5%” and attach a before/after benchmark.

How do I negotiate remote roles from Africa to Europe without a work permit?

Target companies with EOR (Employer of Record) partners like Remote.com or Deel. Many EU startups use EOR to hire globally; the contract is with the EOR entity, not your local company. The salary band will be lower than direct EU hires, but still 2–3× your local rate. Ask about EOR explicitly in the first interview.

Is it worth moving to a tech hub like Berlin or Lisbon first?

Not necessarily. Many global roles are fully remote and don’t require relocation. If you do relocate, choose a city with good internet, lower rent, and coworking spaces—Lisbon, Barcelona, or Tallinn fit those criteria. Relocation packages are rare; negotiate a home-office stipend instead.

What tools should I use to automate my open-source metrics?

Use GitHub Actions with matrix builds for Node 20 LTS, Python 3.11, and Rust 1.75. Add pytest-benchmark 4.0.0 for Python and criterion.rs for Rust. Publish the results to a static dashboard hosted on Render or Vercel. Automate the dashboard update on every push to main; that keeps your portfolio active even during busy periods.
