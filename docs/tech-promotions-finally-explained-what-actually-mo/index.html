Tech promotions finally explained — what actually moves the needle

Ship 3 high-impact projects that measurably improve team KPIs and get promoted in 12 months — impact summaries, leverage scores, and the metrics that actually move the needle

The one-paragraph version (read this first)

Promotions in tech aren’t about working 80-hour weeks or shipping the most lines of code; they’re about visibility, leverage, and impact that others can’t ignore. You need to make your manager’s job easier by solving problems they care about before they ask, in a way that scales beyond just you. That means writing down decisions so others can repeat them, shipping small features that unblock teams, and turning your work into reusable assets—not just pull requests. I’ve seen engineers get promoted in 6 months by doing this, while others with 5 years of experience stalled because they only optimized for personal output. The difference isn’t raw skill; it’s who you make look good.

Why this concept confuses people

Most engineers think promotions are about technical excellence or long hours. That’s only half the story. In reality, promotions are political and operational. Your manager is evaluated on your growth and the team’s output. If your work doesn’t make their life easier or your impact isn’t visible beyond your immediate team, they have little incentive to advocate for you. I once saw a senior engineer work nights and weekends for a year, delivering complex distributed systems work. When promotion time came, their manager said, “We love your code, but we don’t know what you actually do here.” They hadn’t documented their decisions or connected their work to team goals. The team promoted someone else who had shipped three small but high-leverage features that saved the team 15 hours a week.

Another confusion: people conflate activity with impact. Shipping 20 small features that reduce on-call pages by 30% is more promotable than building one massive system no one else understands. I learned this the hard way at a fintech startup in Mexico. I spent three months building a fraud detection pipeline. It worked beautifully in staging, but when I demoed it to leadership, they asked, “So what? How does this help us reduce chargebacks or cut support tickets?” I hadn’t framed it in business terms. Meanwhile, a peer ran an experiment that reduced false positives by 22% and saved $40k in monthly chargebacks. They got promoted. My pipeline was later adopted—but by then, it was too late for me.

Finally, people assume promotions are fair. They’re not. They’re based on perceived value and risk. If you’re the only one who can debug a critical system, you’re a single point of failure—not a candidate for promotion. I once inherited a legacy payments service in Colombia. The previous engineer had built it without tests or runbooks. Every outage required a 3 a.m. call. I spent two weeks writing integration tests, documenting runbooks, and automating rollbacks. Within a month, the on-call rotation dropped from 10 pages a week to 2. My manager told me, “You just made yourself replaceable—exactly what we need for you to scale into a higher role.” Six months later, I was promoted to Tech Lead.

The mental model that makes it click

Think of your career as a product. You’re the engineer, your manager is the product manager, and the company is the market. Your product is “your impact.” To get promoted, you need to increase the perceived value of your product while reducing the risk of depending on you.

Your perceived value comes from three things:

  1. Impact visibility: How clearly can others see the effect of your work?
  2. Leverage: Does your work scale beyond you? Can others use it or learn from it?
  3. Risk reduction: Do you make the team safer, faster, or more predictable?

Your personal leverage score is the number of people who can do your job with minimal hand-holding. If only you can do it, your score is 1. If three teammates can run it, your score is 3. Promotions happen when your leverage score rises above 1 and your impact is visible.

I used this model at a Brazilian marketplace. I built a feature flag system using LaunchDarkly. At first, I was the only one who could deploy flags safely. My leverage score was 1. I spent two weeks writing a CLI tool that wrapped the LaunchDarkly API, added runbooks, and ran a lunch-and-learn. Within a month, four engineers could deploy flags independently. My leverage score jumped to 5. My manager noticed. Six months later, I was promoted to Staff Engineer.

A concrete worked example

Let’s say you’re a mid-level engineer at a SaaS company in Colombia. Your goal is to get promoted to senior within 12 months. Here’s a step-by-step playbook that worked for me and two colleagues.

Month 0–1: Identify the promotion criteria

You need to know the bar. At my last company, the criteria for senior were:

  • Impact: Deliver 3+ projects that measurably improve team KPIs (latency, error rate, revenue, support tickets).
  • Leverage: Document decisions, automate repetitive tasks, and enable 2+ teammates to perform your work.
  • Visibility: Present your work in team meetings and write a quarterly impact summary for your manager.
  • Collaboration: Proactively help other teams; get 2+ cross-team shoutouts in Slack.

I got this wrong at first. I assumed the bar was about code quality. I spent months refactoring a legacy service. My manager said, “The code is great, but we promoted someone who reduced our AWS bill by $12k/month by optimizing a single database query.” I had optimized for the wrong metric.

Month 1–3: Ship small, high-leverage wins

Pick a small, painful problem. Solve it in a way that others can reuse. For example:

  • Problem: The on-call rotation gets paged 15 times a week for API timeouts.
  • Solution: Write a lightweight circuit breaker using Python’s pybreaker library. Add metrics to Grafana. Document the setup in a runbook.

Code:

from pybreaker import CircuitBreaker
import time

# Configure the circuit breaker
breaker = CircuitBreaker(fail_max=3, reset_timeout=60)

@breaker
def call_api(url):
    # Simulate API call
    time.sleep(0.5)
    return {"status": "ok"}

# Usage in a Flask endpoint
@app.route('/health')
def health():
    try:
        return call_api("https://api.example.com/health")
    except Exception as e:
        return {"error": str(e)}, 503

Result: PagerDuty pages dropped from 15 to 3 per week in two weeks. I wrote a 5-minute Loom video explaining the setup and emailed it to the team. Two teammates adopted it within a week. My leverage score went from 1 to 3.

Month 3–6: Connect your work to business metrics

Your next project should tie to a business KPI. For example:

  • Problem: The checkout flow has a 4% drop-off rate at the payment step.
  • Solution: Add a retry mechanism for failed payments using Stripe’s idempotency keys. Measure impact with Segment and Amplitude.

Code:

export const retryPayment = async (paymentIntentId, maxRetries = 3) => {
  for (let i = 0; i < maxRetries; i++) {
    try {
      const paymentIntent = await stripe.paymentIntents.retrieve(paymentIntentId);
      if (paymentIntent.status === 'succeeded') {
        analytics.track('payment_retry_success', { paymentIntentId });
        return paymentIntent;
      }
    } catch (error) {
      analytics.track('payment_retry_failure', { error: error.message });
      await new Promise(resolve => setTimeout(resolve, 1000 * (i + 1)));
    }
  }
  throw new Error('Payment retry failed after max attempts');
};

Result: Payment success rate increased from 96% to 98.2% over six weeks. I wrote a one-pager with graphs and shared it in the #growth Slack channel. The VP of Product replied with a 👍. My manager noticed.

Month 6–9: Build reusable assets and mentor others

Now, make your work reusable. Write a CLI tool, a Terraform module, or a template repo. For example:

  • Problem: Every team deploys infrastructure manually, causing drift and outages.
  • Solution: Build a shared Terraform module for deploying Redis clusters. Add automated tests and CI.

Code:

# modules/redis-cluster/main.tf

Result: Five teams adopted the module in two months. I ran a 30-minute workshop. My leverage score hit 8. My manager told me, “You’re not just shipping code—you’re shipping processes that scale the team.”

Month 9–12: Package your impact

Write a promotion packet. Include: - A list of projects with measurable outcomes (e.g., “Reduced API latency from 500ms to 200ms, cutting support tickets by 18%”). - Testimonials from peers and partners (e.g., “Kevin’s Redis module saved us 8 hours of debugging last sprint”). - A short slide deck or document summarizing your impact.

How this connects to things you already know

If you’ve ever optimized a SQL query, you’ve already used a version of this mental model. A well-optimized query doesn’t just run faster; it reduces load on the database, improves user experience, and lets the frontend team iterate faster. That’s leverage. You didn’t just write a query; you unblocked others.

Similarly, if you’ve ever written a script to automate a manual process, you’ve practiced risk reduction. You made the team safer by removing a single point of failure. The same principle applies to promotions: you’re not just automating tasks; you’re automating your own path to the next level.

I once reduced a nightly batch job from 3 hours to 20 minutes. The immediate benefit was faster reporting. The hidden benefit was that analysts could run ad-hoc queries during the day instead of waiting overnight. My manager cited this in my promotion review as “enabling business agility.”

The key takeaway here is that the skills you use to optimize code or automate tasks are the same skills that get you promoted—if you frame them the right way.

Common misconceptions, corrected

Misconception 1: “Promotions are about writing the best code.”

Correction: Promotions are about making the team better. I’ve seen brilliant engineers who wrote beautiful, maintainable code get passed over for promotion because their work didn’t measurably improve the team’s KPIs. At a Colombian edtech startup, an engineer built a distributed logging system that handled 10k events/sec with zero downtime. It was elegant. But the team’s biggest pain point was onboarding new engineers. The engineer hadn’t documented the system or written tutorials. The team promoted someone who wrote a 30-minute onboarding guide and reduced the time to first PR from 3 days to 8 hours. Code is table stakes; impact is the ante.

Misconception 2: “You need to be the smartest person in the room.”

Correction: You need to be the person who makes everyone else look smart. I once joined a team where a junior engineer was struggling with a complex CI/CD pipeline. I spent two hours pair-programming with them. The next week, they shipped a feature that reduced deployment time by 60%. My manager noticed and said, “You didn’t just solve a problem—you built a teammate.” Promotions reward collaboration, not solo genius.

Misconception 3: “Visibility means bragging.”

Correction: Visibility means clarity. I used to write long, detailed emails about my work. No one read them. Then I started writing one-page impact summaries with: - The problem - The solution - The metric (e.g., “reduced failed payments by 12%”) - A testimonial (e.g., “Saved us 15 hours of debugging last week”)

I sent these to my manager and cc’d my skip-level manager. Within three months, my manager started forwarding my summaries to leadership with comments like “This is the kind of impact we need more of.” Visibility isn’t about self-promotion; it’s about making your impact undeniable.

Misconception 4: “Promotions are fair.”

Correction: Promotions are negotiated. Your manager has a budget and a headcount. If you’re not pushing for the role, they won’t invent it for you. At a Mexican SaaS company, I assumed my manager would advocate for me. I was wrong. When promotion time came, they said, “We don’t have a headcount for senior.” I pushed back: “I’ve shipped X, Y, and Z, and my impact summary shows $40k in cost savings and 20% faster deployments. Can we create a role?” They agreed to a stretch promotion. Fairness is a myth; leverage is reality.

The advanced version (once the basics are solid)

Once you’ve mastered the basics—ship small wins, connect to metrics, build leverage—you’re ready for the advanced playbook. This is where you start shaping the narrative of your career, not just reacting to it.

Step 1: Build a personal brand inside the company

Your personal brand isn’t about Twitter or LinkedIn; it’s about how people describe you when you’re not in the room. I once worked with an engineer who was known for “making the impossible possible.” How? By shipping a feature in two weeks that the PM had said would take three months. They didn’t do it by working 80-hour weeks; they did it by breaking the feature into thin vertical slices and automating the testing pipeline. The story spread. When promotion time came, their manager didn’t even ask for a packet—they just said, “Everyone knows what [Engineer] delivers.”

To build your brand:

  • Tell stories, not features. Instead of “I built a payments retry system,” say, “I cut failed payments by 12% in six weeks, saving the company $40k/month—here’s how.”
  • Create artifacts. A runbook, a CLI tool, a template repo—something others use daily.
  • Be the go-to for a domain. If you own the observability stack, you’re the person others ask for help. That’s leverage.

Step 2: Negotiate your promotion proactively

Don’t wait for your manager to bring it up. Schedule a promotion conversation six months before the next cycle. Bring data:

  • Your impact summary (projects, metrics, testimonials)
  • A proposal for the role (what you’ll deliver in the next 6–12 months)
  • A comparison to the promotion criteria for the role

I did this at a Brazilian marketplace. My manager said, “We don’t have a headcount.” I replied, “I’ve delivered $120k in cost savings and reduced on-call pages by 70%. Can we create a Tech Lead role focused on reliability?” They agreed to a six-month trial. Six months later, I was promoted.

Step 3: Leverage cross-team projects

The fastest path to senior is often through a cross-team project that solves a problem everyone cares about. For example:

  • Problem: The data team’s ETL pipeline breaks weekly, delaying reports.
  • Solution: Build a self-healing pipeline with automatic retries and Slack alerts.
  • Impact: Pipeline uptime went from 85% to 99.5%, saving the data team 10 hours/week.

I led a project like this at a Colombian fintech. The CTO noticed and said, “You didn’t just fix the pipeline—you made the entire org more data-driven.” Six months later, I was promoted to Staff Engineer. The key was solving a problem outside my team’s immediate scope.

Step 4: Master the art of the “no”

Promotions reward people who say yes to high-impact work—but only if they can deliver. Saying yes to everything is a fast track to burnout and stalled growth. Instead, say yes strategically:

  • Yes to projects that build leverage. Automate a manual process. Document a critical system. Build a template.
  • No to projects that make you a bottleneck. If you’re the only one who can debug a system, you’re not promotable—you’re trapped.

I once said yes to building a custom auth system. It took three months. When I was asked to maintain it, I realized I’d become a single point of failure. I spent two weeks writing tests, runbooks, and automating deployments. Then I said no to new auth work until others could take it over. Six months later, I was promoted.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if I’m ready for a promotion?

Look at the promotion criteria for the role you want. If you’ve delivered 3+ projects that measurably improved team KPIs, built leverage (documented decisions, automated tasks, mentored teammates), and made your impact visible to your manager and skip-level, you’re ready. I thought I was ready after one big project. My manager said, “You need to show you can scale your impact beyond a single project.” I added a second project focused on enabling others, and the promotion went through.

What if my manager doesn’t advocate for me?

Don’t assume your manager will advocate for you just because you’re doing good work. Promotions are negotiated. Schedule a conversation six months before promotion cycles. Bring your impact summary, a proposal for the role, and a comparison to the promotion criteria. I once worked with an engineer who assumed their manager would advocate for them. When promotion time came, they were passed over. They pushed back and got a stretch promotion six months later. The key is to make it easy for your manager to say yes.

How do I handle it if I’m told there’s no headcount?

If there’s no headcount, ask: “What would it take to create a role?” Frame your request in terms of business impact. For example, “I’ve delivered $120k in cost savings and reduced on-call pages by 70%. If we create a Tech Lead role focused on reliability, I can reduce outages by another 30% in six months.” I used this approach at a Brazilian marketplace. My manager agreed to a six-month trial. Six months later, I was promoted. The key is to tie your ask to business outcomes, not just your career growth.

Why does visibility matter if my work speaks for itself?

Your work doesn’t speak for itself—people forget. I once built a system that reduced AWS costs by $40k/month. When promotion time came, my manager said, “I don’t remember the specifics of your work.” I hadn’t framed it in terms of impact. The next quarter, I wrote a one-page impact summary with metrics and testimonials. My manager cited it in my promotion review. Visibility isn’t about bragging; it’s about making your impact undeniable.

Final step: Ship your first impact summary today

Open a blank document. Write three bullet points:

  1. A problem you solved in the last 30 days.
  2. The metric that improved because of your work (e.g., “reduced failed payments by 12%”).
  3. A testimonial from a peer or partner (e.g., “Saved us 15 hours of debugging last week”).

Save it. Next week, send it to your manager with the subject line “30-day impact summary.”

This isn’t about asking for a promotion—it’s about making your impact visible. Once you do this consistently, promotions become a conversation, not a negotiation.

Start today. Your next role is waiting for someone who makes the team better.
