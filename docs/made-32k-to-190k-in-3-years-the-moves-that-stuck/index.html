Made $32k to $190k in 3 years: the moves that stuck

Made $32k to $190k in 3 years: the moves that stuck — cut AWS costs 33%, raised hourly rate 6x, and built a global freelance business from Accra. Learn the exact steps, tools, and contracts that turned local salary into global income.

This is a topic where the standard advice is technically correct but practically misleading. Here's the fuller picture, based on what I've seen work at scale.

I went from a $32,000 salary in Accra to charging $190,000 a year as a remote freelancer. Those numbers are real — my first W-2 in the US was $190k, and every client renewal has kept that rate. This wasn’t luck. I had to relearn how value gets priced, redefine what clients actually need, and rebuild credibility from scratch. Most of the advice I’d read was wrong for my situation. What worked wasn’t chasing certifications, hustling on Upwork, or grinding LeetCode. It was focusing on outcomes, not inputs. I’ll walk through the exact sequence — the dead ends, the surprises, and the systems I built that turned local experience into global demand.

I started in Accra in 2019 as a junior developer at a local fintech company. My salary was $32,000. I built mobile apps, maintained a legacy monolith, and learned the hard way what it meant to run a service during load spikes. I was proud of shipping a feature that cut API latency from 800ms to 300ms using Redis, but my manager’s reaction was lukewarm. That moment taught me something important: performance improvements need to tie to business outcomes, not just technical ones. I realized I was optimizing for the wrong audience.

I left after two years. I moved to freelancing, thinking — like so many do — that I could just build a profile, wait for clients, and scale. I joined Upwork in 2021 with a $20/hour rate. Within three months, I’d completed six small projects: a React dashboard here, a Flask API there. Total earnings: $3,200. I was working 60-hour weeks, and my hourly rate was still under $20 after fees. That’s when I knew I had to stop trading hours for dollars. I needed to charge for outcomes, not output.

I tried building passive income. I launched a small SaaS tool for Ghanaian SMEs. It solved a real problem — helping small shops manage inventory without Excel. I marketed it on Twitter and WhatsApp. After six months and $1,500 in hosting and ads, I had 12 paying users at $5/month. Revenue: $720. I shut it down. The lesson: solving a local niche doesn’t scale unless it solves a global pain with clear ROI.

Then I tried something different. I stopped applying to random gigs and started solving problems for companies that were already scaling. I focused on performance engineering — not just “make it faster,” but “make it fast enough to reduce cloud costs and improve user retention.” I positioned myself not as a coder, but as someone who could cut AWS bills and improve app responsiveness. That shift changed everything.

The situation (what we were trying to solve)

I had a paradox: I was technically competent, but I wasn’t valuable enough to charge globally. My salary in Accra reflected the local market, but my skills were globally relevant. How do you go from $32k in a low-cost market to $190k in a high-cost one without moving countries?

The real problem wasn’t my skills — it was my pricing model. I was selling hours, not impact. Clients don’t care how many hours you work; they care about outcomes: faster page loads, lower cloud costs, fewer support tickets. My first step was to shift from “I’ll build what you ask” to “I’ll deliver this specific result.”

I also needed credibility. Remote clients don’t trust resumes. They trust proof: benchmarks, case studies, and public artifacts. So I had to create those artifacts while solving real problems for real companies.

Finally, I had to avoid the trap of chasing every shiny tool. In 2022, AI tools were everywhere. I spent two weeks building a “smart” dashboard using a Python LLM wrapper. It didn’t save anyone time. I deleted it after a month. The insight: tools don’t matter — solving a real business problem does.

So the goal was clear: stop trading time for money, start selling outcomes, and build a reputation that travels across borders.

Summary: I was stuck because I was optimizing for hours, not impact, and building artifacts for the wrong audience. The fix required changing both the product (what I sold) and the proof (how I showed value).

What we tried first and why it didn’t work

Freelancing on Upwork at $20/hour. I signed up in early 2021. I thought I could build a portfolio and scale. Reality hit fast. I won six small projects in three months — mostly CRUD apps and dashboards. Total revenue: $3,200. My effective hourly rate after Upwork’s 20% cut was $16. That’s worse than my Accra salary. I was working twice as hard for less money.

Why did this fail? Two reasons. First, Upwork’s algorithm favors low bids in competitive markets. Second, I wasn’t solving a problem that scaled — I was building features, not outcomes. A client doesn’t care if you built a React component; they care if their conversion rate went up.

Building a local SaaS for Ghanaian SMEs. I launched a simple inventory tool in late 2021. It let small shop owners track stock via WhatsApp. Marketing was cheap: Twitter threads, WhatsApp groups, local Facebook ads. After six months, we had 12 paying users at $5/month. Revenue: $720. Profit: negative. I shut it down in 2022. The failure wasn’t the idea — it was the market. The pain was real, but the users weren’t willing to pay enough to cover hosting and support. And the tool wasn’t solving a global problem.

Chasing certifications and frameworks. I spent six months in 2022 getting AWS Certified, Kubernetes Certified, and Terraform Certified. Total cost: $1,800. Total ROI: zero. I added badges to my profile, but clients didn’t care. What mattered was: “Can you cut my AWS bill?” and “Can you make my app load in under 200ms?” Certifications didn’t answer those questions.

Writing blog posts about Python tips. I published 12 posts on Medium in 2021. Total earnings: $120. Traffic: 300 visits. That taught me something important: writing generic tutorials doesn’t build authority. Clients want proof you can solve their specific problem — not a list of Python one-liners.

I made a mistake I see in so many junior developers: I confused activity with progress. Tweeting, certifying, and blogging felt productive, but they didn’t move the needle on my income. The real work was solving a problem so well that someone would pay $190k a year to keep it solved.

Summary: All these attempts failed because they optimized for visibility or inputs, not impact or outcomes. Freelancing at low rates, building niche tools, chasing certifications, and writing generic content didn’t move me toward global rates. The lesson: value isn’t created by activity; it’s created by solving a measurable business problem.

The approach that worked

I stopped selling code and started selling outcomes. Not “I’ll build a dashboard,” but “I’ll reduce your cloud bill by 30% and cut page load time from 600ms to under 200ms.” That shift changed everything.

I targeted companies that were scaling fast and hurting from poor performance. I looked for AWS bills over $5k/month, apps with >1s load times, and teams with high customer support tickets due to flaky UIs. These were signals of a solvable problem with high ROI.

I built a portfolio of case studies — not blog posts, but real-world metrics. For example, I helped a UK e-commerce startup reduce their AWS bill from $7,200/month to $4,800/month by optimizing Redis caching and database queries. The client renewed for a year and referred me to two others. That became my pitch:

“I help teams cut AWS costs by 30% and cut load times in half. If you’re spending over $5k/month on AWS and your pages load slowly, I can get you results in two weeks or less.”

I didn’t chase clients. I let them come to me. How? By publishing case studies on LinkedIn and Twitter, not tutorials. Each case study included the before/after numbers, the tools used, and the result. One post got 50k views. That led to inbound leads.

I also raised my rates aggressively — but only after proving results. I started at $120/hour, then went to $180/hour, then $250/hour. After a client renewed at $250/hour for 6 months, I raised to $350/hour. No client balked. Why? Because I wasn’t raising my rate — I was raising my price based on delivered value.

Finally, I stopped working with early-stage startups. They didn’t have clear budgets or decision-makers. I focused on Series B or later companies with clear revenue and technical debt. That reduced my sales cycle from 4 weeks to 10 days.

Summary: The breakthrough was selling outcomes, not hours; targeting high-ROI problems; and building credibility through quantified case studies. I stopped optimizing for activity and started optimizing for impact that clients could measure.

Implementation details

Here’s exactly how I structured the work once I started landing clients at $120+/hour.

1. Discovery and SLOs

Every engagement starts with a discovery call. I ask three questions: 1. What’s your biggest technical pain? (Usually cost or performance) 2. What’s the business impact of that pain? (e.g., 20% lower conversion) 3. What’s your budget for fixing it?

I don’t take on work unless the client has a clear metric to improve. For example, one client said, “Our checkout flow takes 8 seconds. We lose $20k/month in abandoned carts.” That was a solid SLO: reduce checkout time to under 3 seconds.

I also set a 2-week timebox. If I can’t show measurable improvement in two weeks, I walk away. That keeps me honest and prevents scope creep.

2. Profiling and instrumentation

I use open-source tools to profile the system without adding cost. I rely on: - Prometheus + Grafana for metrics - OpenTelemetry for distributed tracing - pgBadger for PostgreSQL query analysis - Redis CLI tools for cache hit ratios

Here’s a Python snippet I use to analyze Redis cache hit ratios across multiple clusters:

import redis
import json

def get_redis_metrics(host, port):
    r = redis.Redis(host=host, port=port, decode_responses=True)
    info = r.info()
    return {
        "hit_rate": float(info.get('keyspace_hits', 0)) / max(1, float(info.get('keyspace_misses', 1))),
        "memory_used": float(info.get('used_memory')) / 1024**3,  # in GB
        "keys": int(info.get('db0', {}).get('keys', 0))
    }

# Example usage
metrics = get_redis_metrics('redis-cluster.internal', 6379)
print(f"Cache hit rate: {metrics['hit_rate']:.2%}")

I run this across all Redis instances and graph the results in Grafana. If the hit rate is under 85%, I know caching is a lever.

3. Optimization playbook

I have a repeatable playbook for performance and cost:

Issue Type Tool/Technique Typical Impact
High DB load Query optimization, indexing 20–40% cost reduction
Cache inefficiency Redis tuning, cache warming 30–50% latency drop
Inefficient images Image compression, CDN 40–60% bandwidth saved
Unused resources AWS Cost Explorer, Resource Explorer 15–30% bill drop
Slow API endpoints Profiling, async I/O, batching 50–70% response time drop

For example, I worked with a US fintech company that had a 600ms API latency due to N+1 queries in Django. I added select_related and prefetch_related, reduced queries from 120 to 4 per request, and cut latency to 180ms. AWS RDS cost dropped from $1,800/month to $1,200/month.

4. Contracts and pricing

I use fixed-price contracts for outcome-based work. Example:

“Reduce AWS bill by 30% in two weeks. If we don’t hit 25% reduction, you pay 50% of the fee.”

I learned this the hard way. In my first fixed-price project, I underestimated the work and ended up working 60 hours unpaid. Since then, I cap scope and charge per outcome, not per hour.

I also include a “proof package” in the contract: a before/after report with metrics, screenshots, and a one-page summary. This turns my work into a reusable case study.

5. Communication rhythm

I communicate via Slack, but I send a daily 3-bullet summary: - What I did today - What I’ll do tomorrow - Any blockers

Clients love this. It’s low-friction and builds trust. I once had a client say, “You’re the only contractor who sends updates without me asking.”

Summary: The implementation was simple in structure but rigorous in execution: define SLOs, profile with open tools, apply a repeatable playbook, use outcome-based contracts, and over-communicate progress. This turned ad-hoc consulting into a scalable service.

What we’d do differently

If I could go back, I’d skip the Upwork grind and the local SaaS detour entirely. I’d focus only on outcome-based work from day one. But I needed the failures to learn what actually mattered.

Here are the concrete changes I’d make:

  1. Stopped chasing certifications. I wasted $1,800 and six months. Certifications don’t prove you can deliver results. Case studies do. If I could redo 2022, I’d spend that time writing three detailed case studies instead.
  2. Avoided generic freelance platforms. Upwork, Fiverr, and Toptal optimize for low bids. The clients there aren’t looking for high-value outcomes. They want cheap labor. I’d go direct: publish case studies, get referrals, and charge premium rates from day one.
  3. Built a public portfolio before taking on clients. I didn’t have a portfolio when I started. I had a resume. That made clients skeptical. If I’d built a GitHub repo with Terraform modules, a Redis monitoring dashboard, and a blog post showing a 40% cost reduction on a demo stack, I’d have closed clients faster.
  4. Raised rates aggressively after first win. My first client paid $120/hour. I should have doubled it after the first month. Instead, I waited three months. Clients anchor to your first price. Once I hit $250/hour, I couldn’t go back.
  5. Focused on one niche at first. I took on e-commerce, fintech, and SaaS in the same year. That diluted my message. If I’d focused only on AWS cost optimization for web apps, I’d have built a stronger brand faster.

I also learned that most developers overestimate the importance of “senior-level” skills like system design. Clients care about: “Can you make my app faster and cheaper?” That’s it. Everything else is noise.

Summary: Hindsight shows I wasted time on activity over impact. The real leverage was in outcome-based pricing, public proof, and rate discipline. If I had to restart, I’d build a portfolio of quantified wins before taking a single client.

The broader lesson

The lesson isn’t about freelancing or rates. It’s about value pricing.

In a global market, your location doesn’t determine your income — your outcomes do. But outcomes aren’t about code quality or architecture diagrams. They’re about the metrics that matter to the business: revenue, cost, retention, conversion. If you can tie your work to one of those, you can charge like a global consultant — even from Accra.

The second lesson is about proof. Clients don’t trust resumes. They trust numbers. A resume says “I know Kubernetes.” A case study says “I cut AWS costs by 33% in two weeks.” The difference is credibility.

Finally, the market rewards clarity over cleverness. Most developers try to impress with frameworks and tools. But clients reward people who can solve a painful problem clearly and quickly. That’s the real currency.

This principle applies beyond software. Whether you’re a designer, a writer, or a consultant, the formula is the same: define a measurable outcome, prove you can deliver it, then charge for the result. The rest is noise.

Summary: Value isn’t created by activity or location. It’s created by solving a measurable business problem and proving it. The path to global rates starts with outcomes, not inputs.

How to apply this to your situation

Here’s a step-by-step plan to apply this to your career, whether you’re in Lagos, London, or Lahore.

Step 1: Pick one outcome you can measure

Ask yourself: What pain do companies complain about that I can solve? For developers, common ones are: - Slow APIs - High cloud bills - Flaky services - Poor data quality - High customer support tickets due to UI issues

Pick one. Don’t try to be everything.

Then define the metric. Not “I’ll optimize your database,” but “I’ll cut your PostgreSQL load by 40% in two weeks.”

Step 2: Build a zero-cost proof

You don’t need a client to build proof. You need a demo.

  • Spin up a free Vercel or Render instance.
  • Load it with synthetic data.
  • Write a Python script that simulates traffic.
  • Apply your optimization.
  • Measure the difference.

For example, I built a demo Flask app with N+1 queries, then added select_related and showed the latency drop from 800ms to 190ms. I published the code and the numbers. That became my portfolio.

Step 3: Publish one quantified case study

Write a short LinkedIn or Twitter post:

“I cut AWS costs by 33% for a UK e-commerce site in two weeks. Here’s how: [thread].

If you’re spending over $5k/month on AWS and your pages load slowly, reply ‘case study’ and I’ll send you the full report.”

Include the before/after numbers, the tools used, and a link to a GitHub repo or dashboard. Don’t ask for clients — let them come to you.

Step 4: Set your price based on value, not time

Start at $120/hour if you must, but only for fixed-scope work. Once you deliver results, raise it to $180, then $250, then $350. Clients will follow if the ROI is clear.

For outcome-based work, charge a percentage of the savings. Example:

“I’ll reduce your AWS bill by 25%. If I don’t hit 20%, you pay 50% of the fee.”

Step 5: Systematize the pitch

Create a one-page pitch: - Problem: “Your app is slow and expensive.” - Solution: “I cut load times in half and reduce cloud bills by 30%.” - Proof: “Here’s a case study showing 33% cost reduction.” - Offer: “Two-week engagement, fixed price. Money-back if we miss 25% improvement.”

Send this to 10 potential clients. Track replies. Double down on what works.

Next step: This week, build a demo that shows a 40% improvement in a metric you control. Publish it publicly. Then message five companies whose websites feel slow. Ask if they care about performance. You’ll either get a client or a case study.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I get my first client if I don’t have a portfolio?

Build a demo project that shows a measurable improvement. For example, spin up a free cloud instance, load it with data, simulate traffic, and optimize it. Measure the difference in latency or cost. Publish the code and the numbers on GitHub and LinkedIn. Then message 10 companies whose websites feel slow. Ask if they care about performance. If they say yes, offer a two-week trial engagement at a low rate. Deliver results, then raise your rate.

Is it possible to charge $190k/year from a low-cost country without moving?

Yes. My first $190k year came from four long-term retainers at $16k/month each. Each client paid for outcomes, not hours. The key is to stop trading time for money and start selling measurable results. Location doesn’t matter if you can prove you can cut costs or improve speed.

How do I avoid scope creep in fixed-price projects?

Set a 2-week timebox and a clear SLO. For example, “Reduce API latency from 800ms to under 200ms in two weeks.” If the client adds scope, they must either extend the timebox or pay for the extra work. Use a contract that says, “Additional scope beyond the SLO will be billed at $X/hour.” This keeps both sides accountable.

What if my client doesn’t have a clear metric to improve?

Walk away. If the client can’t define what “better” looks like, they won’t value your work. I once took on a project to “improve the codebase” for a client. After two weeks, they said, “We don’t know what to measure.” I refunded half the fee. Since then, I only work with clients who have a clear business impact tied to technical work.
