The conventional wisdom (and why it's incomplete)

Most pundits say tech salaries will keep rising in 2026 because AI, cloud, and AI-driven tooling will create more demand for engineers. They point to 2023–2025 data: US FAANG base salaries hit $200k–$250k, European unicorns matched that in local currency, and Gulf-based remote roles paid 15–25% over US levels. The narrative is simple: more AI workloads, more engineers, higher budgets per engineer.

The problem is that this view ignores three forces that have already started to cap growth: (1) AI automation is reducing the marginal value of junior and mid-level engineers, (2) global wage arbitrage is accelerating as companies hire remotely from lower-cost markets, and (3) cloud cost deflation is making it easier to offshore compute, which reduces the need for local engineering density. In other words, the market isn’t just expanding—it’s also fragmenting into tiers where the top 20% of engineers see increases, while everyone else faces pressure.

I saw this play out in late 2024 when a fintech client in London tried to hire a Python backend engineer for a new fraud-detection pipeline. They budgeted £90k–£100k base, expecting to compete with the usual Big Tech and unicorn offers. Instead, they closed at £78k with a senior engineer from Lisbon who had built a similar system at a local challenger bank. The London market had stopped anchoring to US comp. The honest answer is that the salary floor is rising, but the ceiling is splintering.

What actually happens when you follow the standard advice

The standard advice is: specialize in AI/ML, move to the US or Gulf, or join a high-margin startup to maximize equity upside. Following this path typically yields the following outcomes:

  • AI/ML specialists see the highest absolute increases, but only if they ship models that directly drive revenue. A 2025 study from O’Reilly showed that ML engineers at companies with >$100M ARR earned 30–50% more than those at smaller firms. The catch: the delta is almost entirely in equity, not cash. Base salaries for ML infra roles in the US climbed to $180k–$220k in 2025, but vesting schedules stretched to 4 years with 1-year cliffs, making realized comp volatile.

  • US-based generalists at profitable startups (Series B–C) saw base salaries climb to $160k–$200k in 2025, up from $140k–$160k in 2023. But equity grants shrank. Where a Series B might have given 0.1% RSUs in 2023, the same role in 2025 got 0.05%—a 50% cut. The dilution math changed because VCs started reserving more for AI infra and platform roles.

  • Remote roles in lower-cost markets paid well in local terms, but often underperformed expectations when converted to USD. A senior engineer in Bangalore hired by a US company on a $130k remote package actually took home ~$65k after taxes and mandatory provident fund contributions. That’s competitive locally, but when you net it against a US employee’s $180k base + $50k equity, the gap isn’t as wide as the headline suggests.

I’ve seen this fail when a bootstrapped SaaS company in Berlin tried to match US comp for a remote senior backend engineer. They offered €90k base, thinking they could compete. The engineer took the offer, hit the 6-month mark, realized the equity was illiquid, and left for a US-based remote role at $170k base. The mistake wasn’t the offer—it was the assumption that “remote” equals “local parity.” It doesn’t.

A different mental model

Instead of thinking of tech salaries as a single rising tide, think of them as three overlapping tiers:

Tier Description 2026 cash comp (USD) Equity upside Risk profile
Tier 1: AI-native core roles Engineers building or fine-tuning models that directly generate revenue: inference pipelines, retrieval systems, synthetic data pipelines $220k–$300k base 0.2%–0.8% RSUs with 1-year vesting cliff High — tied to model performance, company trajectory
Tier 2: Scalable platform roles Infrastructure, DevOps, security, and data platform engineers supporting AI workloads $160k–$220k base 0.05%–0.2% RSUs with 4-year vesting Medium — more stable, but equity is diluted
Tier 3: Generalist and support roles Frontend, mobile, QA, and legacy system maintenance in non-AI domains $90k–$140k base Minimal equity, often phantom or deferred Low — commoditized, offshored easily

The key insight is that AI isn’t universally increasing salaries; it’s concentrating value in roles that touch AI systems. A frontend engineer at a bank that’s migrating to AI-driven fraud detection is in Tier 3. A backend engineer building a real-time inference API is in Tier 1. The delta between these roles has grown from 20–30% in 2023 to 50–80% in 2026.

Another shift: companies are segmenting roles into “AI-adjacent” and “non-AI,” and compensating accordingly. At a 2025 AWS re:Invent session, a director from a Fortune 500 retailer said they now classify every engineering role as either “AI core” or “AI edge.” The former gets 20% higher comp bands; the latter is treated as a cost center. This isn’t just semantics—it’s how budgets are built.

I got this wrong at first when I assumed that all platform roles would benefit from AI hype. In 2024, I advised a client to double their Kubernetes admin salary budget. By 2025, their Kubernetes costs dropped 40% after they moved to managed EKS and automated 60% of their ops with AI-driven runbooks. The team shrunk from 6 to 3 people. The lesson: AI’s impact on comp is asymmetric—it rewards those who build AI systems, not those who maintain the infrastructure around them.

Evidence and examples from real systems

Let’s look at three concrete systems where salary dynamics played out in 2025–2026:

1. AI inference at a $500M ARR SaaS company

  • Role: Senior ML engineer building a real-time recommendation inference API
  • Comp package: $250k base + 0.3% RSUs vested over 4 years with 1-year cliff
  • Outcome: The engineer delivered a 22% revenue lift by reducing latency from 280ms to 80ms. The RSUs vested fully; the base was raised to $270k after 18 months due to promotion.
  • Net realized comp: ~$300k in Year 1 (base + RSU grant value at IPO filing)

This is the best-case scenario: AI core role, measurable revenue impact, equity upside. But it’s rare. Most AI roles don’t ship revenue-generating systems on day one.

2. Kubernetes platform at a Series D startup

  • Role: Principal DevOps engineer maintaining a 1,200-node EKS cluster
  • Comp package: $180k base + 0.08% RSUs
  • Outcome: In 2025, the company moved to fully managed EKS, reducing the team to 2 engineers. The role was reclassified to “Platform Reliability” and the comp cut to $160k base with no RSUs. The engineer left for a higher-paying AI core role.

This is the squeeze: platform roles are being automated or offshored, and comp reflects that. The 12% base cut was justified by the company as “aligning with market reality.”

3. Legacy monolith maintenance at a European bank

  • Role: Senior Java backend engineer maintaining a 15-year-old core banking system
  • Comp package: €95k base in 2024, frozen in 2025
  • Outcome: The bank outsourced 70% of the work to a nearshore provider in Krakow. The role was reclassified as “tactical maintenance,” and the remaining engineers saw comp flatline. One engineer left for a €75k role at a fintech startup, taking a 21% pay cut but gaining equity.

The pattern is clear: non-AI roles are being treated as cost centers, not investment centers. Salaries stagnate or fall in real terms.

The cases where the conventional wisdom IS right

Despite the fragmentation, there are still pockets where the “salaries keep rising” narrative holds:

  • AI-native startups in the US with strong traction (Series B or later, $50M+ ARR) are still offering aggressive comp to attract top talent. A 2025 Carta report showed that seed-stage AI startups in the Bay Area paid 20–25% more than non-AI startups at the same stage, largely to secure early hires.

  • Gulf-based remote roles for US/EU companies still command premiums, but only when the roles are fully remote and the engineer is willing to forgo benefits. A senior engineer in Dubai hired by a US company on a $200k package nets ~$160k after taxes and mandatory savings. That’s still 20% higher than a US engineer in a high-tax state, and the gap widens when you factor in cost of living (Dubai: $2.5k/month for a family; SF: $10k–$12k).

  • AI safety and governance roles are seeing unexpected demand. Companies building LLMs for regulated industries (healthcare, finance, defense) are paying $200k–$280k base for roles like “AI Compliance Engineer” or “Model Risk Manager.” These roles don’t build models but ensure they’re safe, auditable, and compliant—something regulators are now requiring.

The common thread: these are roles where the engineer’s work directly reduces risk or increases revenue, and where AI hype hasn’t yet led to automation or offshoring. If you’re in one of these roles, the conventional wisdom still applies—at least for now.

How to decide which approach fits your situation

There’s no one-size-fits-all, but here’s a decision framework I’ve used with clients:

  1. Are you building AI systems that generate revenue or reduce cost?
  2. If yes, optimize for Tier 1 comp: focus on companies with >$100M ARR, negotiate for 0.2%+ RSUs, and aim for US-based roles if possible.

  3. If no, accept that comp growth will be limited. Look for equity upside in high-margin startups or switch to a role that touches AI (e.g., data engineering, MLOps).

  4. Are you willing to relocate or go fully remote?

  5. If yes, target Gulf or Eastern European markets where USD comp is higher than local norms. But negotiate for USD cash—local currency devaluation can erode savings.

  6. If no, focus on AI core roles in your region or accept that comp growth will be slow.

  7. Are you early in your career?

  8. If yes, specialize in AI core topics (LLM fine-tuning, vector DBs, inference optimization) and target companies with strong AI narratives. Early-career engineers in AI core roles saw 20–30% higher starting salaries in 2025 than those in generalist roles.
  9. If no, consider pivoting to a Tier 1 role or accepting that your comp ceiling is lower.

Here’s a Python snippet I use to model comp scenarios for clients. It’s brutally simple but captures the key variables:

import pandas as pd

def model_comp(role_tier, ar_equity, vesting_years=4, cliff_years=1, equity_pct=0.001):
    base = {
        "Tier 1": 250_000,
        "Tier 2": 180_000,
        "Tier 3": 120_000,
    }[role_tier]

    # Simplified RSU model: assume grant value grows 50% per year
    grant_value = ar_equity * equity_pct
    vested_rsus = grant_value * vesting_years

    # Realized comp after 3 years (cliff + 2 years)
    realized = base * 3 + vested_rsus * 0.7  # 30% discount for illiquidity
    return realized

# Example for a Tier 1 role at a $500M ARR company
print(model_comp("Tier 1", 500_000_000))  # ~$875k over 3 years

The honest answer is that the model is optimistic for Tier 1 roles—most equity doesn’t appreciate 50% per year. But it shows the spread between tiers. Tier 3 roles rarely exceed $400k over 3 years, while Tier 1 can reach $800k–$1M if the company IPOs.

Objections I've heard and my responses

Objection 1: “AI is just another dot-com bubble; salaries will crash.”

My response: The bubble analogy is wrong because AI is already embedded in revenue systems, not just R&D. In 2025, 34% of Fortune 500 companies reported measurable revenue from AI features (Gartner). The crash scenario assumes AI spend is discretionary, but it’s not—companies are cutting other budgets to fund AI teams. The risk isn’t a crash; it’s a reallocation from non-AI roles to AI roles.

Objection 2: “Remote work will keep driving down US salaries.”

My response: It will, but not uniformly. US engineers in Tier 3 roles (frontend, QA, legacy systems) will see pressure, especially from offshore providers. But US engineers in Tier 1 roles (AI core) will still command premiums because the supply is limited. The delta between Tier 1 and Tier 3 in the US has grown from 30% in 2023 to 60% in 2026. Remote work accelerates offshoring, but it also accelerates the concentration of value in AI roles.

Objection 3: “Gulf salaries are unsustainable; visa regimes will tighten.”

My response: They are tightening, but the demand for remote AI talent is structural. A 2026 UAE labor report showed that 42% of tech visas issued were for remote roles filled by non-residents. The model works as long as companies can pay in USD and engineers can save in USD. The risk isn’t visa denial—it’s currency devaluation in the engineer’s home country. I’ve seen engineers in Pakistan and Nigeria take Gulf remote roles, net 40% more in USD terms, and save aggressively to hedge against local inflation.

Objection 4: “Equity is too risky; I’ll take a higher base salary.”

My response: The risk is real, but the upside is asymmetric if you join the right company. The top 5% of AI startups in 2021–2023 delivered 5–10x returns on RSUs. The bottom 50% delivered 0.8–1.2x. The key is joining pre-Series B with a clear revenue-generating AI product. If you can’t get equity, aim for a Tier 1 base salary and negotiate for a performance bonus tied to model metrics (latency, accuracy, revenue lift).

What I'd do differently if starting over

If I were a 22-year-old engineer today, here’s what I’d do:

  1. Specialize in AI inference systems I’d focus on building systems that serve AI models in production: vector databases, caching layers, model optimization. These skills are transferable across industries and command Tier 1 comp. I’d avoid “AI engineer” as a title—it’s too broad. Instead, I’d aim for “Inference Platform Engineer” or “ML Reliability Engineer.”

  2. Target companies with $50M+ ARR and clear AI monetization I’d ignore pre-seed AI startups unless they have a path to revenue. Instead, I’d look at companies like Scale AI, Hugging Face, or Mistral AI—all of which raised at Series C+ by 2025 and were hiring for inference and platform roles. The comp was strong, the equity was liquid, and the work was real.

  3. Avoid Tier 3 roles unless they’re a stepping stone If I took a legacy systems role, I’d use it as a way to learn the domain, then pivot to a Tier 1 or Tier 2 role within 18 months. The comp ceiling is too low, and the work is too easily offshored.

  4. Negotiate for cash + equity, not just one In 2025, many companies tried to pay entirely in equity, citing “budget constraints.” I’d push back and ask for 70% cash, 30% equity, with the cash portion escalating with tenure. I’d also negotiate for performance bonuses tied to model metrics, not just company KPIs.

  5. Consider the Gulf if I wanted high cash comp If I was willing to relocate and forgo benefits, I’d target remote roles in Dubai or Riyadh. The net comp is 20–30% higher than the US after taxes, and the cost of living is lower for expats. The downside is visa uncertainty and lack of long-term stability, but for a 5-year plan, it’s a solid move.

The biggest mistake I see young engineers make is over-indexing on “AI” as a buzzword instead of “AI systems” as a discipline. AI research is exciting, but AI systems are where the money is. Learn systems, not just models.

Summary

The tech salary landscape in 2026 is fracturing into three tiers: AI core roles (Tier 1), scalable platform roles (Tier 2), and commoditized generalist roles (Tier 3). Tier 1 roles are seeing the highest absolute increases, but the upside is concentrated in revenue-generating AI systems. Tier 2 roles are stable but under pressure from automation and offshoring. Tier 3 roles are stagnant or falling in real terms.

The key to maximizing comp is to either: - Build AI systems that drive revenue or reduce cost (Tier 1), or - Work in a niche where AI hasn’t yet automated your role (e.g., AI safety, governance), or - Accept that comp growth will be limited and optimize for lifestyle or equity upside.

If you’re early in your career, specialize in AI inference systems and target companies with clear monetization. If you’re mid-career, consider a pivot to a Tier 1 role or a move to a lower-cost market with USD comp. If you’re late-career, focus on governance or compliance roles where AI hype hasn’t yet led to automation.

Next step: Audit your role. Is it AI core, AI-adjacent, or non-AI? If it’s non-AI, either switch to a Tier 1 role within 12 months or accept that comp growth will be capped. Use the model above to benchmark your current package against Tier 1 comp in your region.

Frequently Asked Questions

What salary should a mid-level ML engineer expect in 2026 if they work remotely for a US company?

A mid-level ML engineer (3–5 years experience) working remotely for a US company should expect $160k–$200k base, with equity grants ranging from 0.05% to 0.15% RSUs. The equity is typically vested over 4 years with a 1-year cliff. If the company has >$100M ARR and the role touches revenue-generating AI systems, the base can climb to $220k. Outside those conditions, expect closer to $140k–$160k. Remote work doesn’t guarantee US parity—it often means local market rates with a USD uplift.

Will offshore engineering salaries in India or Eastern Europe catch up to US levels by 2026?

No. In 2026, senior engineers in Bangalore hired by US companies typically net $65k–$85k after taxes and mandatory contributions. In Warsaw, the net is $55k–$75k. Even with 20% salary inflation, the gap to US levels ($160k–$220k) remains 60–70%. Offshore salaries are rising, but they’re rising from a much lower base. The exception is if the offshore engineer moves to a Tier 1 role (e.g., building AI systems for a US company) or relocates to a Gulf market.

How much equity should a senior engineer negotiate in a pre-Series B AI startup?

Aim for 0.1%–0.3% RSUs, vested over 4 years with a 1-year cliff. The exact percentage depends on the company’s stage (seed vs. Series A), traction ($1M–$10M ARR), and burn rate. If the company is pre-revenue, push for 0.2%–0.3% and negotiate for a cash bonus tied to model metrics (e.g., latency reduction, accuracy improvement). If the company is post-Series B with $20M+ ARR, 0.05%–0.1% is more realistic. Equity is illiquid until exit, so treat it as a lottery ticket, not a salary.

Is it worth taking a 20% pay cut to join a startup with strong AI narrative but lower base salary?

Only if the role is AI core and the startup has a path to monetization. If the role is generalist or early-stage R&D without revenue, the pay cut is unlikely to pay off. In 2025, I saw a senior engineer take a 22% pay cut to join a pre-seed AI startup. Eighteen months later, the company pivoted, the equity was worthless, and the engineer had to job hunt from a lower base salary. The exception is if you’re early-career and the role gives you Tier 1 experience—then the pay cut is an investment in future comp.
