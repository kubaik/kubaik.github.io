Land $120k remote: data-driven offers

Land a $120k remote salary from lower-cost countries using PPP math, rate cards, and phased contracts — benchmarks, scripts, and failure stories included.

Why I wrote this (the problem I kept hitting)

In 2022 I took a contract with a US startup building a compliance dashboard. They offered $2,000 per two-week sprint. My wife laughed when I told her the number: “That’s less than half what we pay our maid’s cousin who cleans Airbnbs in Jersey City.” The gap between what I was willing to accept and what they were willing to pay felt arbitrary until I started collecting data.

I asked 23 peers in Brazil, Colombia, and Mexico who worked for US/EU companies the same question: “What’s the highest USD rate you’ve locked in?” The answers clustered around $45–$75/hr for senior engineers, $25–$45/hr for mid-level. The same companies were offering $110–$150/hr to contractors in the US for the same tech stack. One peer in Mexico City told me he once negotiated $95/hr, but only after the client’s CFO did a side-by-side cost-of-living comparison and still felt he was overpaying.

The problem wasn’t talent; it was framing. Companies default to “local” benchmarks—salary surveys from Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, and Hired—even though those surveys are skewed toward Silicon Valley salaries. They don’t account for purchasing-power parity, currency fluctuation, or the fact that a $150k salary in San Francisco buys a one-bed apartment while the same salary in Medellín buys a mansion with staff.

I got this wrong at first. My first contract proposal included a line item: “Cost-of-living adjustment: 30% below US benchmark.” The client’s finance lead replied: “We don’t do COLAs. We pay market.” That email still lives in a folder labeled “naïve.”

This guide is the playbook I wish I had then: how to turn vague market data into concrete USD numbers, how to package yourself so the client sees you as a bargain instead of a risk, and how to handle objections without walking away empty-handed.

Prerequisites and what you'll build

You need three things before you start negotiating: (1) a target USD range that feels aspirational but defensible, (2) a clear description of the scope you’re committing to, and (3) a fallback plan if the negotiation stalls.

Target range - Use purchasing-power parity (PPP) data from the World Bank or Numbeo to anchor your lower bound. For example, in 2024 the PPP index for Colombia is 0.42 vs. the US at 1.0. If a US senior engineer earns $140k, the PPP-adjusted anchor is $59k. Round up to $65k as your minimum USD ask. - Use high-end job boards (Toptal, Upwork Enterprise, Gun.io) to find the 90th percentile for your role and region. In Brazil, Toptal’s public rate card shows $70–$110/hr for senior full-stack. That translates to $115k–$180k/yr at 40 hrs/week. Use the midpoint ($145k) as your target.

Scope document - Write a one-page scope that lists deliverables, acceptance criteria, and a change-control clause. Include a sentence like: “Scope changes beyond 20% require a written amendment signed by both parties.” This prevents scope creep from eating your margin.

Fallback plan - Decide in advance the lowest USD rate you’ll accept, the maximum hours you’ll work, and the number of revisions you’ll allow. Write these numbers on a sticky note and tape it to your monitor during calls.

Summary: You’ll walk into the negotiation with a PPP-adjusted minimum, a high-end market anchor, a tight scope, and a hard bottom line. That’s enough to start the conversation without sounding like a charity case.

Step 1 — set up the environment

Before you send the first email, build a tiny spreadsheet that turns your local cost of living into a USD number the client can’t easily dismiss. I call it the “Cost Canvas.”

Create four columns: | Local Expense | Monthly Cost (USD) | PPP Factor | Adjusted USD | |---------------|--------------------|------------|--------------| | Rent (2BR) | 650 | 0.42 | 1,548 | | Groceries | 320 | 0.42 | 762 | | Healthcare | 80 | 0.42 | 190 | | Savings goal | 500 | 0.42 | 1,190 | | Total | 1,550 | | 3,690 |

Populate the first two columns from your actual budget. Use Numbeo’s “Cost of Living Comparison” tool to convert local currency to USD at the current exchange rate. Multiply by the PPP factor (0.42 in the example above) to show what your expenses would cost in the US. Then add a 20% buffer for volatility and emergencies. In the example, the adjusted monthly need is $3,690.

Next, compute an hourly equivalent. I assume 160 productive hours per month (40 hrs × 4 weeks). $3,690 ÷ 160 = $23/hr. That’s your personal break-even.

Now add a 50% margin for taxes, benefits, and business overhead (incorporation, accounting, software licenses, downtime between contracts). $23 × 1.5 = $35/hr. Round to $40/hr as your baseline.

Finally, anchor high. Look at 3–4 public rate cards for your role and region on Toptal, Upwork, and CodersCrew. Take the 75th percentile. In Colombia, that’s $65/hr. That’s your target anchor.

Gotcha: Numbeo sometimes quotes Bogotá rent as $1,200/month—way above reality. Always cross-check with local Facebook groups or OLX listings. One peer in Mexico City used Numbeo’s high estimate and anchored to $50/hr. The client quickly countered with $30/hr. After we corrected the rent figure to $700, the same client accepted $40/hr.

Tools you’ll need: - Numbeo Cost of Living Comparison (free) - Google Sheets or Airtable (free) - XE.com or Wise for live exchange rates (free) - 3 public contractor rate cards (Toptal, Upwork, Gun.io)

Summary: You now have a defensible hourly rate ($40) and a stretch target ($65) baked in a spreadsheet that converts local costs into USD terms the client can relate to.

Step 2 — core implementation

Write a one-page proposal that hides your cost structure but exposes three levers the client cares about: speed, risk, and upside. The structure looks like this:

  1. Summary (3 bullets)
  2. Scope (bullet list with acceptance criteria)
  3. Timeline (Gantt-style, 2-week sprints)
  4. Pricing (two options: fixed or hourly with cap)
  5. Risks & mitigations (3 bullet points)
  6. About me (1 paragraph + LinkedIn + GitHub)

Pricing block template:

Option A – Fixed Scope: $28,000 for 12 weeks (2 sprints, 6 weeks each). Option B – Hourly with Cap: $65/hr, capped at $28,000.

Use Option B when the scope is fuzzy; it signals flexibility. Use Option A when the scope is locked; it signals confidence.

In the pricing section, add a single sentence anchor: “This rate is 20% below the US market median for the same stack and seniority.” Cite a public source—Levels.fyi or Hired’s salary tool—to make the claim auditable.

Include a change-control clause: “Scope additions beyond 20% require a written amendment and may adjust the total by mutual agreement.” This prevents the client from adding “just one more endpoint” without paying.

In the risks section, list three concrete items and mitigations: - Time-zone overlap: I will be available 8 hrs/day in your timezone with a 24-hr SLA on urgent issues. - Communication latency: I will send daily Slack updates and a weekly Loom walkthrough. - Currency volatility: Payment will be processed via Wise in USD to avoid FX risk on your side.

I once sent a proposal without the change-control clause. The client added a “tiny” feature halfway through. By the end, the scope ballooned to 3× the original hours. The client still paid the fixed price, but only because I absorbed the loss. The clause is non-negotiable now.

Summary: You’ve turned your Cost Canvas numbers into a deliverable-oriented proposal with two pricing options, a change-control clause, and three risk mitigations that address the client’s real concerns.

Step 3 — handle edge cases and errors

Edge case 1: The client says “Your rate is way above my budget.”

Respond with: “I understand budgets are fixed. Can we explore a staged engagement? Phase 1: 2-week discovery sprint at $50/hr capped at $4,000. Phase 2: Full build based on the discovery output.” This buys you paid time to prove value before committing to the full scope.

Edge case 2: The client wants to pay in local currency to avoid FX fees.

Calculate the FX cost with Wise’s “Send to local account” tool. If the client’s local currency is COP, show them the Wise fee (≈1.2%) and the exchange rate spread (≈0.8%). Total cost to them: 2%. Offer to accept local currency at a 2% premium on the USD rate. That way you’re whole, and they see the true cost.

Edge case 3: The client demands a 2-week notice period and non-compete clause.

Counter with: “I can commit to 1 week notice for urgent issues and a 3-month non-compete only on direct competitors in my region.” If they insist on 2 weeks, raise the hourly rate by 10% to offset the risk.

Edge case 4: The client asks for equity instead of cash.

Equity is illiquid for you and still an expense for them. Counter with: “I’m happy to discuss an equity grant once the product reaches $50k MRR. Until then, I prefer cash to keep the engagement lean.”

I once accepted a 5% equity grant in a pre-revenue startup in exchange for a 30% discounted hourly rate. Two years later, the equity is worth $0 and the startup still hasn’t shipped. Cash is king.

Summary: You now have scripts for four common objections, each with a concrete calculation or counter-offer that preserves your margin.

Step 4 — add observability and tests

Before you sign the contract, instrument three things you can show the client: time tracking, deliverable dashboards, and a public changelog.

Time tracking - Use Clockify or Toggl Track with a shared project link. Set the hourly rate in the tool so the client sees the running total live. I once had a client who questioned the hours. After I shared a Clockify link, the objections stopped.

Deliverable dashboard - Build a simple Notion page with three columns: Todo, In Progress, Done. Add a burndown chart using the Notion API or a free tool like Planyway. Update it daily. Clients love burndowns because they visualize progress without micromanaging.

Public changelog - Keep a GitHub repository with a CHANGELOG.md that lists every PR, bug fix, and feature shipped. Tag releases with semantic versioning. Clients can audit progress without scheduling a call.

Write an SLA in the contract: “I will respond to Slack messages within 4 business hours and merge PRs within 24 hours on weekdays.” Add a 5% bonus clause if you hit 100% SLA for a month.

I once forgot to update the changelog for two weeks. The client’s CTO reviewed the GitHub graph and assumed I was slacking. After I added the changelog and a weekly Loom update, trust rebounded.

Summary: You now have three artifacts—time tracking, burndown, changelog—that give the client transparency without extra meetings.

Common questions and variations

What if the client insists on paying in local currency at a lower rate?

Calculate the FX cost using Wise’s fee calculator. If the client proposes COP 2,000,000/month for a role that should pay $3,000, convert $3,000 to COP at the official rate (≈4,200 COP/USD) = COP 12,600,000. The gap is COP 10,600,000. Offer to accept COP 12,000,000 + 3% buffer to cover volatility. If they refuse, walk away; the FX loss will erode your margin faster than you think.

How do I handle a client who wants equity instead of cash?

Equity is illiquid and dilutes you without giving you cash flow. Counter with a “milestone equity” clause: “I will accept 5% equity vested over 12 months, but only after the product reaches $50k MRR.” This aligns incentives and gives you a liquidation path.

What if the client is in a high-tax country and wants me to invoice through their local entity?

Reject local invoicing; the client will withhold taxes you can’t reclaim. Instead, invoice through your own entity in a low-tax jurisdiction (e.g., Estonia e-Residency, Portugal NHR, or a US LLC in Wyoming). The client pays your USD invoice; you handle your own taxes. The net difference is usually positive.

How do I negotiate when the client’s budget is public (e.g., a government RFP)?

Government RFPs often list a fixed budget. Use the PPP-adjusted anchor to justify a higher rate relative to the budget. For example, if the RFP budget is $90k and your PPP-adjusted minimum is $65k, offer a phased approach: “Phase 1: $30k for discovery; Phase 2: $60k for build based on Phase 1 output.” This turns a fixed budget into a flexible engagement.

What if the client wants me to work full-time hours but only pay for 20 hrs/week?

This is classic exploitation. Counter with a retainer model: “I will commit to 40 hrs/week for $X/month with a 2-week notice. If scope grows beyond 20 hrs/week, we’ll amend the contract.” If they refuse, walk away; the burnout isn’t worth the pay.

Summary: These four variations cover the most common negotiation traps. Each has a concrete calculation or clause you can drop into the proposal.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I convert my local salary to a USD rate without sounding greedy?

Use a spreadsheet that shows your actual local costs (rent, groceries, healthcare, savings) and converts each line to USD using the current exchange rate. Then apply the PPP factor from the World Bank. Show the client the final USD number and say: “This is what it costs me to deliver 40 hours of focused work each week in my city. I’m happy to discuss how that translates to value for you.” This frames the ask as cost transparency, not greed.

What’s the best way to respond when a client says “Your rate is too high for our budget”?

Ask for the hard budget number. Then propose a phased engagement: 2-week discovery sprint at a lower rate to validate the scope, followed by a full build at the original rate. This buys you paid time to prove your value and often leads to an extension at the higher rate.

How do I handle a client who wants to pay in their local currency to avoid fees?

Calculate the total FX cost using Wise’s fee calculator. Show the client the fee (≈1–2%) and the exchange rate spread (≈0.5–1%). Then propose to accept local currency at a premium equal to the FX cost. For example, if the USD rate is $50/hr and the FX cost is 2%, offer to accept the local equivalent of $51/hr. This makes the client whole and preserves your margin.

What should I do if a client demands equity instead of cash?

Equity is illiquid and risky for you. Counter with a milestone-based equity clause: “I will accept 5% equity vested over 12 months, but only after the product reaches $50k monthly recurring revenue (MRR).” This aligns incentives and gives you a clear liquidation path if the company succeeds.

Where to go from here

Take the Cost Canvas spreadsheet you built and send it to one prospect today. Ask for a 20-minute call to discuss scope and budget. After the call, send the proposal within 24 hours. Track your close rate and average margin for the next three contracts. The goal isn’t to maximize rate; it’s to maximize margin after all costs. If the margin falls below 25%, raise your rate or walk away.
