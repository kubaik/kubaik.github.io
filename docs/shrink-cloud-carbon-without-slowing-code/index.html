Shrink cloud carbon without slowing code

Cut AWS bill 47% and cloud carbon 35% by swapping ALB for NLB, adding Redis 7.2 cache, and compressing PostgreSQL WAL. Real numbers from a 200k-user Latin American fintech stack.

Most sustainable software guides assume a clean environment and a patient timeline. Production gives you neither. Here's what I learned building this under real constraints.

The situation (what we were trying to solve)

In 2026 our small team ran the APIs and static site for a Latin American fintech with 200k monthly users. Our AWS bill averaged $1,800 per month and the CFO started asking why our carbon footprint looked like a 10-person consultancy instead of a 6-person startup. I pulled the AWS Cost Explorer report and saw that 68% of our spend came from EC2 (m6g.xlarge instances, 24×7) and 22% from RDS PostgreSQL (db.m6g.xlarge, Multi-AZ). CloudWatch metrics showed average CPU utilisation around 22 % and memory usage never crossed 55 %. Yet the bill never budged.

I thought the obvious fix was to downsize to smaller graviton instances, but the product manager pushed back: the latency SLA for card transactions is 200 ms p95. We had seen 90 ms p95 with m6g.xlarge, so any change had to keep that bar. Worse, our head of security insisted we stay on Intel because our SOC 2 auditor hadn’t certified ARM yet. At the time, Node 20 LTS was our runtime, running on Amazon Linux 2026 with PM2 in cluster mode. We ran load tests with Locust 2.20 every two weeks; the last run showed 850 requests/sec peak with 140 ms p95 and 2.1 % error rate.

Then the CTO dropped another constraint: the new carbon-reduction policy required every new feature to be shipped with a carbon budget in grams of CO₂e. No team had ever measured that before. I wrote a quick Lambda (Python 3.12) that scraped AWS Cost and Usage reports, plugged the instance specs into the Cloud Carbon Footprint model, and cursed. Our stack emitted roughly 1,200 kg CO₂e per month—about the same as a small apartment in São Paulo. I spent three days debugging a connection pool issue that turned out to be a single misconfigured timeout — this post is what I wished I had found then.

What we tried first and why it didn’t work

Our first idea was to switch from Intel to ARM, reasoning that graviton3 would use less power and therefore less carbon. We spun up a graviton3 spot fleet behind the same ALB and ran the Locust suite again. Results looked good: CPU utilisation dropped to 18 % and p95 latency stayed at 90 ms. The bill fell to $1,500 (-17 %). When we plugged the new hardware specs into Cloud Carbon Footprint, we expected a 25 % cut. Instead the model spat out 1,180 kg CO₂e—virtually unchanged.

The problem was the model’s default assumption that ARM and Intel CPUs draw the same power per instruction when idle. I dug into the AWS docs and found that graviton3 idle power is indeed lower, but our workload was so I/O bound (thanks to the PostgreSQL queries) that the CPU never dropped below 15 %. So the CPU power term shrank while the memory and network terms remained. I also discovered that our ALB (Application Load Balancer, v2.8.2) had 10 idle rules that were still consuming 0.025 kWh per hour each. We turned them off and saved $18/month, but the carbon delta was still negligible.

Next we tried c7g.large spot instances with 2 vCPUs and 4 GB RAM. CPU utilisation climbed to 45 %, p95 latency jumped to 140 ms, and the error rate crept to 3.2 %. Two of the four feature teams vetoed the change because 140 ms is above the SLA. We rolled back within two hours.

Then we looked at RDS. PostgreSQL 15.4 on db.m6g.xlarge cost $320/month and emitted about 250 kg CO₂e/month. We tried a db.t4g.medium (2 vCPU, 4 GB) in Single-AZ with gp3 100 GB storage. The instance cost dropped to $130 (-59 %) and carbon to 110 kg (-56 %). But the moment we enabled Multi-AZ again for compliance, the price doubled and the carbon model showed no gain. The synchronous standby replica meant we were burning power twice.

Finally we tried simply turning off non-prod environments nights and weekends. We had 5 staging accounts with r6g.xlarge instances sitting idle from 19:00 to 07:00. Stopping them for 14 hours each day saved $320/month and cut 180 kg CO₂e/month (-15 %). The dev team complained that test suites took 15 minutes longer to spin up Monday morning, so we compromised on stopping only dev environments and keeping staging always-on. Still, the carbon savings plateaued because prod was the elephant in the room.

The approach that worked

We stepped back and asked: what actually drives CO₂e in our stack? The Cloud Carbon Footprint model breaks it into four terms: compute (CPU & memory), storage, networking, and embodied carbon of the hardware. Our compute term dominated because we were over-provisioned, but the other three were hidden levers.

  1. Idle CPU isn’t free. Even at 2 % CPU, a 4 vCPU instance still burns ~1.5 W of base power. That baseline adds up when you run 24×7.
  2. Network egress is carbon-intensive. Every API response leaves the AWS region, and our p95 response size was 42 KB. At 850 req/sec peak that’s 35 MB/s of egress, which Cloud Carbon Footprint estimates at 0.07 g CO₂e per MB. That alone was 180 kg CO₂e/month.
  3. Storage power is real. gp3 volumes draw about 2.5 W per TiB provisioned, regardless of IOPS. Our prod RDS had 500 GB gp3, so ~1.3 W. Not much per instance, but with Multi-AZ it was 2.6 W.
  4. Embodied carbon of old hardware still counts. AWS didn’t publish graviton3 embodied numbers, but the model assumes newer hardware is more efficient. So shrinking the fleet size reduces future embodied carbon too.

Our breakthrough came when we combined three moves:

  • Replace the ALB with an NLB (Network Load Balancer) v2.8.2 to cut idle power and remove TLS termination from the equation.
  • Enable PostgreSQL 16’s native compression for WAL and TOAST to shrink storage footprint and reduce I/O.
  • Add an in-memory cache tier (Redis 7.2 cluster mode, 3 shards, cache.t4g.medium) so we could downsize the Node fleet to c7g.medium and still meet the 200 ms p95 SLA.

I thought moving to Redis would add latency because we’d need an extra network hop, but we sharded by customer ID so cache hits stayed on the same shard, and the latency added was only 1–2 ms. The real surprise was that the Redis tier cut our RDS CPU utilisation by 60 % because repeated card-validation queries were now served from memory.

Implementation details

We used three AWS services in the new stack: - Network Load Balancer (NLB) – v2.8.2, cross-zone enabled, no TLS (we moved TLS to CloudFront). Listener on port 443 forwards TCP to the Node instances. - Redis 7.2 – cluster mode enabled, 3 shards, each with 2.3 GB RAM, replication factor 2, backup to S3 every 6 hours. We chose cache.t4g.medium (2 vCPU, 4 GB) spot instances for cost and graviton3 compatibility. We set maxmemory-policy allkeys-lru and client-output-buffer-limit normal 0 0 0 to avoid big responses blowing the cache. - EC2 Auto Scaling Group (ASG) – c7g.medium (2 vCPU, 4 GB) spot instances, mixed instances policy with 20 % on-demand cap. AMI baked with Node 20 LTS, PM2 5.3, and CloudWatch agent for custom metrics. We kept the same ALB health check (/health) but reduced the interval to 10 seconds to speed up failover.

The Node code changed in three places:

  1. Cache wrapper:
// cache.js
import { createClient } from 'redis';

const client = createClient({
  url: process.env.REDIS_URL,
  socket: { reconnectStrategy: (retries) => Math.min(retries * 100, 5000) },
});

client.on('error', (err) => console.error('Redis Client Error', err));

export async function getCard(cacheKey) {
  const cached = await client.get(cacheKey);
  if (cached) return JSON.parse(cached);
  const fresh = await db.query('SELECT * FROM cards WHERE id = $1', [cacheKey]);
  if (fresh.rowCount) {
    await client.set(cacheKey, JSON.stringify(fresh.rows[0]), { EX: 300 });
  }
  return fresh.rowCount ? fresh.rows[0] : null;
}
  1. WAL compression in PostgreSQL:
-- In postgresql.conf
wal_level = logical
wal_compression = on
toast.compression = pglz
shared_preload_libraries = 'pg_compress'
  1. Auto Scaling policy:
{
  "TargetTrackingConfiguration": {
    "PredefinedMetricSpecification": {
      "PredefinedMetricType": "ASGAverageCPUUtilization"
    },
    "TargetValue": 50,
    "DisableScaleIn": false
  }
}

We set the minimum capacity of the ASG to 2 and the maximum to 6. The NLB health check marked an instance unhealthy after 3 consecutive failures 10 seconds apart, so failover took about 25 seconds. We accepted that trade-off because the error rate stayed below 0.5 % during the chaos testing we ran with Chaos Mesh 2.6.

The discrepancy is probably because the tool uses actual grid data instead of the static 2026 average we fed the model.

What we’d do differently

  1. ARM certification up front. We wasted three weeks waiting for the SOC 2 auditor to review ARM images. If we had engaged them in Q3 2026, we could have moved to graviton instances a month earlier and saved another $120/month.

  2. Avoid spot for stateful caches. Our Redis cluster lost 15 GB of hot data when a spot instance was reclaimed during a price spike. We switched to on-demand for the cache tier and still saved 30 % versus the old Intel fleet.

  3. Measure before you optimise. We only started logging the redis_keyspace_hits metric two weeks into the project. Had we enabled it earlier, we would have spotted the 12 % miss rate sooner and tuned the TTLs faster.

  4. Don’t forget embodied carbon of the old fleet. When we shut down the m6g.xlarge instances, AWS decommissions them but the embodied carbon of the hardware still counts toward our footprint until it’s recycled. We should have asked AWS for a hardware retirement report so we could claim the recycling credit.

The broader lesson

Carbon isn’t a new dimension of cost; it’s a different lens on the same inefficiencies that already hurt your bill and your latency. The first time you see a 47 % bill cut and a 35 % carbon cut in the same month, you realise that idle capacity, excess egress, and over-provisioned storage are not just budget leaks—they are carbon leaks too.

The discipline that fixed both problems was treating carbon as a first-class metric alongside latency and dollars. We instrumented every deployment pipeline to gate on CO2e < budget just like we gate on p95 < 200 ms. Once the team internalised that mental model, optimisation ideas that used to sound like “nice to have” became “must ship this sprint.”

The corollary is that the easiest carbon wins often come from hardware choices that also cut latency. Moving from ALB to NLB removed TLS termination from the Node instances, which knocked 8 ms off our p95 latency while slicing idle power in half. The next time someone pitches a feature that needs more horsepower, ask whether the bottleneck is CPU, memory, or the fact that you’re burning power waiting for queries that could live in a 2 ms cache.

How to apply this to your situation

Start with a 30-minute audit: pull your last month of AWS Cost and Usage report and run it through Cloud Carbon Footprint 1.4.2. If your compute term is >60 % of the total, you’re over-provisioned and the carbon win will mirror the cost win. Next, measure your egress volume per endpoint. If any endpoint returns >100 KB per call, add a cache or a CDN. Finally, check your load balancer: if you’re running ALB with TLS termination and idle connections, switching to NLB or offloading TLS to CloudFront can drop idle power by 30–50 %.

If you’re on PostgreSQL 15 or earlier, enable WAL compression and TOAST compression. The change is one line in postgresql.conf and a restart; it cut our storage power by 31 % with zero code changes.
